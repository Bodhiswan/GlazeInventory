## 12. Resist & Masking

Wax resist works because it blocks water from wetting porous clay or bisque. Glaze and underglaze are water-based suspensions, so if the surface is coated with wax they cannot soak in and bond properly [Ceramic Arts Network, "A Cooler Alternative to Hot Wax Resist"; Mayco AC-302 product page]. This is primarily an absorption-control technique, not a glaze-chemistry reaction.

For Glaze Library's audience, **cold water-based wax emulsions** are the most relevant resist. AMACO and Mayco both sell brushable wax-resist products that work at room temperature, are easier to clean up than molten wax, and are designed for greenware and bisque workflows [AMACO Wax Resist; Mayco Product Discovery Manual, p. 78]. Greenwich House's glaze handbook notes that older paraffin systems were smelly and toxic compared with modern water-based wax products, and also distinguishes **latex** as a useful removable resist when the mask needs to be peeled off rather than burned away [Greenwich House Pottery Glazes and Clay Handbook, p. 42].

Wax resist is most reliable on clean bisque, especially for protecting the foot, preserving negative space, or separating decorative areas. It becomes riskier over unfired glaze. AMACO explicitly warns that wax applied over dipping glazes or glazes without gum can peel the glaze from the ware [AMACO Wax Resist product guidance]. Linda Arbuckle likewise notes that curling wax usually means the bisque was dusty and/or the wax was applied too thickly; she recommends thinning with water and softening the curled area with a hair dryer if needed [Linda Arbuckle, *Majolica and Lowfire Handout*, p. 9]. Digitalfire documents another failure mode: residual wax can contribute to micro-pinholing under low-fire clear glaze over underglaze, meaning decorative wax should be used sparingly and cleaned carefully when a clear finish is planned [Digitalfire, "Wax resist inducing surface pinholes on a low fire transparent over an underglaze"].

**Hard masks** such as tape and shellac are often better than brushed wax when the goal is a very sharp line. Tape, paper, and related barriers are explicitly included in Greenwich House's resist overview [Greenwich House, p. 42]. Shellac-resist water etching is one of the clearest documented systems: AMACO applies underglaze to bone-dry ware, paints shellac over the areas to keep, removes exposed underglaze with a wet sponge, then stresses thorough cleaning before glazing [AMACO, "Water Etching with Velvet Underglaze"]. Tape over raw glaze can work, but the evidence is thinner and the risk of lifting is real if the glaze is not fully dry or well-bound. For the public guide, that should be framed as conditional rather than routine.

## 13. Sgraffito, Mishima, and Raised Decoration

**Sgraffito** works best while the clay is still leather hard and the slip or underglaze has dried to the touch. AMACO's underglaze tutorials consistently use that timing because it gives the tool enough bite to cut a clean line without smearing wet color or tearing brittle dry clay [AMACO, "Enhancing Surface with Velvet Underglazes"; AMACO, "Amphora Project: Heroes and Handbuilding"]. Greenwich House defines sgraffito simply as scratching through slip to reveal the clay beneath [Greenwich House, p. 42], but the practical insight from the tutorials is the timing window: too wet and the edge smears; too dry and the line crumbs and chatters.

**Mishima / inlay** also belongs mainly in the leather-hard stage. The process is to incise, force contrasting slip or underglaze into the linework, allow it to stiffen, then scrape the excess back until the surface is flush [AMACO, "More Colorful Mishima"; Greenwich House, p. 42]. Both GPT and Grok converged on the same important caution: slip is often mechanically safer than commercial underglaze because it can be tuned to match the body's drying and firing behavior more closely. Digitalfire's broader warning about engobes and slips applies directly here: if the inlay layer does not fit the body in shrinkage, thermal expansion, and firing maturity, it can crack, blur, or detach [Digitalfire, "Are Your Glazes Food Safe or are They Leachable?"].

**Slip trailing and raised decoration** require a thicker, more stable material than an ordinary brush coat. The line needs enough viscosity to stand up after application, and enough compatibility with the body to survive drying and firing without cracking off [AMACO, "Slip Decorating Mug with White Slip"; Rhodes excerpts on engobes and trailing]. Raised lines also round off somewhat in the kiln due to surface tension, so sharply embossed effects remain cleaner at low fire or with underglaze/slip than with very fluid mid-fire or high-fire glaze systems.

## 14. Underglaze, Majolica, Washes, and Low-Fire Overglaze

For most commercial-glaze users, **underglaze under clear** is the core decorative system. AMACO Velvet and Mayco Fundamentals both present underglaze as usable on greenware, leather-hard clay, or bisque, with a clear glaze deepening and enriching the final color [AMACO Velvet Underglaze product page; Mayco Fundamentals Underglazes guide]. But the clear layer is not neutral. Digitalfire shows that some underglazes bleed, some lose opacity, and some pink/red systems are especially chemistry-sensitive under certain clear glazes. Chrome-tin colors may need a low-calcium, zinc-free, carefully balanced clear, which is why zinc-free clears so often appear in underglaze tutorials [Digitalfire, "Underglaze"; AMACO, "Water Etching with Velvet Underglaze"]. Thickness matters too: too much clear can blur edges and trap gases; too little can leave the surface underdeveloped or less glossy than intended. Mayco specifically recommends a 15-minute hold in low-fire co-fired clear-over-underglaze work to help burn out residual gases more cleanly [Mayco Fundamentals Underglazes guide].

**Majolica** is a more specialized low-fire decorative system built around an opaque white glaze base. Historically this was tin-opacified, but many modern systems use zircon because it is cheaper and more available [Digitalfire, "Majolica"]. The defining functional property is not simply "white glaze" but a white glaze with enough viscosity to stay in place and preserve decorative brushwork during firing. Arbuckle's handout explains that this restricted flow is why majolica can hold watercolor-like brush decoration without the linework washing away [Linda Arbuckle, *Majolica and Lowfire Handout*, pp. 1-9]. The same chemistry makes majolica sensitive: dusty bisque, over-thick glaze, excess opacifier, or body gases can cause crawling, pinholing, blistering, and other white-surface defects [Arbuckle; Digitalfire, "Majolica"; Ceramic Arts Network, "Decorating Techniques and Troubleshooting Tips for Majolica"]. For the site, majolica should be explained as its own decorative glaze family, not just "painting on top of white."

**Oxide and stain washes** are a different decorative logic again. Greenwich House describes them as watercolor-like colorants that settle into recesses and can be wiped back from raised areas, leaving emphasis in the texture [Greenwich House, p. 38]. The major dividing line is whether the wash is fluxed. A fluxed wash using Gerstley Borate or frit integrates more securely in the firing, while an unfluxed wash may remain dry-looking or less durable [Greenwich House; Ceramic Stains & Glazes stain-mixing guidance]. On functional ware, this becomes a safety issue as well as an aesthetic one. Digitalfire's leaching article is blunt that heavily metal-bearing decorative surfaces, especially copper- and manganese-rich ones, should not be casually assumed food-safe; proven liner glazes on food-contact interiors are the safer route [Digitalfire, "Are Your Glazes Food Safe or are They Leachable?"].

Finally, **overglazes, metallics, and lusters** are not just another brush coat on greenware or bisque. They are separate low-temperature systems applied after the glaze firing. Mayco's overglaze guide places china paint around cone 018, metallics around cone 019-018, and gold/mother-of-pearl lusters around cone 020-018, with specific warnings that underfiring and overfiring cause different failures such as rubbing off, paling, matting, blistering, or staining [Mayco, *Guide to Overglazes*]. That makes them worth mentioning in the guide, but they should stay clearly separated from underglaze-under-clear workflows.

## Editorial Rulings

- Use **cold wax emulsions** as the default resist recommendation for the public-facing guide; mention hot wax and latex only as secondary variants.
- Treat **tape over raw glaze** as conditional, not standard. The stronger sources support tape as a resist category, but the most reliable public guidance should still favor bisque, underglaze, shellac, or well-dried gummed surfaces.
- Emphasize that **underglaze + clear is a compatibility system**, not a guaranteed mix-and-match workflow.
- Keep **majolica** framed as a distinct opaque-glaze decorative method with its own defect profile.
- Keep strong **food-safety caveats** around oxide washes and decorative metal-rich surfaces.
