### [Wax Resist: Hot Wax (Paraffin/Beeswax) vs Cold Wax Emulsion (Commercial like Mayco AC-302 or AMACO)]
**Statement:** Hot wax (paraffin or beeswax) requires heating to molten state for application and is petroleum- or natural-based, producing strong odor and toxicity during use; cold wax emulsions (water-based commercial products like Mayco AC-302 or AMACO Wax Resist) are non-toxic, brushable at room temperature, easier to apply/clean, and burn off cleanly in firing without residue when used properly—preferred for low-fire commercial brush-on glaze users.

### [Wax Resist: Differences in Paraffin, Beeswax, Latex, and Commercial Emulsions (Brushability, Drying, Cleanup, Burnoff, Contamination Risk)]
**Statement:** Commercial emulsions (Mayco AC-302/AMACO) are highly brushable, dry quickly without heating, clean up with water/soap, burn off completely in firing leaving no residue; paraffin requires heat (smelly/toxic fumes), beeswax is natural but slower-drying and more expensive; latex is removable pre-firing for multi-layer work but can leave slight residue if not fully dry; all risk contamination if applied over dusty surfaces or too thick (causing curling/peeling).

### [Wax Resist: When Wax Helps vs When It Causes Defects (e.g., Curling, Crawling, Peeling)]
**Statement:** Wax helps by creating precise barriers for multi-color glazing, foot protection, or negative space designs (burns off leaving clean resist areas); it causes defects if applied over dusty bisque (curling/peeling) or too thick (crawling in glaze), or if used over gumless dipping glazes (peels entire coat); always thin with water if curling occurs and heat gently with hair dryer to flatten.

### [Tape and Masking Methods: Clean-Line Masking on Bisque vs Glazed Surfaces]

### [Sgraffito Through Slip/Underglaze/Glaze: Timing Windows and Leather-Hard vs Bisque Differences]

### [Mishima/Inlay: Fill-and-Scrape Sequence, Slip vs Underglaze, Shrinkage Mismatch Risks]
**Statement:** Mishima (inlay) involves incising lines at leather-hard, filling with contrasting slip or underglaze, allowing to dry, then scraping excess with a metal rib or sponge for flush surface; slip matches body shrinkage better than commercial underglaze (reducing cracking/blurring under clear glaze); mismatch risks blurring or cracking if underglaze shrinks differently.
**Source:** AMACO resources (Mishima tutorial references); Greenwich House Pottery Glazes and Clay Handbook PDF (p. 42: "MISHIMA: see inlay... lines scratched in clay and filled with colored clay, slip or glaze").

### [Majolica/Opaque White Base with Decoration: Why It Works, Common Defects and Control, Brushwork]
**Statement:** Majolica uses a viscous tin- or zircon-opacified white glaze base that does not flow much in firing, preserving brushwork line quality and allowing watercolor-like color washes to "sink in" without extensive bleeding; defects include crawling from thick application or dusty bisque, pinholing from body or glaze gases, and white dotting or surface disturbance from opacifier-related bubbling; control comes from thinner even application, properly prepared bisque, and slower firing where needed.

**Statement:** Oxide and stain washes enhance texture by settling into recesses and being wiped back from high spots; fluxed washes that include a borate or frit integrate more securely in the glaze firing, while unfluxed washes can remain dusty, unstable, or more prone to leaching depending on oxide choice and firing; food-surface use requires testing because copper, manganese, and other metal-bearing decorations may not be chemically durable.
