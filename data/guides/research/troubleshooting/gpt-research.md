# GPT Research — Troubleshooting (Part VI)

Phase A | Researched 2026-04-09

### Crawling Can Start Before the Kiln Even Gets Hot
**Statement:** Hamer describes crawling as a defect that often begins with poor dry-glaze adhesion before firing: dust, grease, overhandling, or firing ware while it is still wet from glazing can loosen the coat so that when the glaze melts it pulls into islands instead of staying attached to the body.
**Source:** Frank and Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 3rd ed., p. 89 (OCR text via StudyLib result already opened in session)
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web OCR source review.
**Notes:** This is a useful “why” explanation missing from many beginner lists: a lot of crawling is seeded during drying, not only during melt.

### Thickened Glaze Slop and Viscous Melts Make Crawling Worse
**Statement:** Hamer also notes that a glaze slurry that has thickened by evaporation, or a glaze that is very viscous when molten, is more likely to crawl because the melted glaze prefers to pull together rather than wet the body.
**Source:** Frank and Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 3rd ed., p. 89 (OCR text via StudyLib result already opened in session)
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web OCR source review.
**Notes:** This supports thinning or reconditioning the slurry before changing the recipe.

### High-Alkali Glazes Can Both Run and Crawl
**Statement:** Rhodes notes that highly alkaline glazes can be short in firing range and have high surface tension when melted, which makes them vulnerable both to running and to crawling.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, p. 227 (Google Books snippet result for “crawling” / “running”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This is a useful chemistry link: the same recipe can be fluid enough to run yet still have poor wetting behavior.

### Crazing Is Primarily a Fit Problem, Not a Firing-Curve Problem
**Statement:** Digitalfire is explicit that the real cause of most crazing is thermal-expansion mismatch between glaze and body; firing changes may delay visible cracking, but if the fit is wrong they do not solve the underlying problem.
**Source:** Tony Hansen, “Glaze Crazing,” Digitalfire, https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct webpage review.
**Notes:** This slightly qualifies some studio advice that emphasizes slow cooling or thinner application as if they were primary cures.

### Crazing Seriously Weakens Functional Ware
**Statement:** Digitalfire reports measured strength losses of roughly 300-400% in freshly fired ware with crazed glazes, because the crack network acts as pre-existing failure initiation sites.
**Source:** Tony Hansen, “Glaze Crazing,” Digitalfire, https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct webpage review.
**Notes:** This is one of the strongest practical reasons not to dismiss crazing as merely cosmetic on functional work.

### Delayed Crazing Is Common on Porous Earthenware Because the Body Keeps Expanding
**Statement:** Hamer explains that porous earthenware bodies can absorb atmospheric moisture and expand permanently over time, while the glaze does not; the delayed tension then produces crazing even if the ware came from the kiln apparently sound.
**Source:** Frank and Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 3rd ed., p. 89 (OCR text via StudyLib result already opened in session)
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web OCR source review.
**Notes:** This is a classic explanation for delayed crazing that Claude only mentioned briefly.

### Shivering Is the More Dangerous Opposite of Crazing
**Statement:** Hamer states plainly that shivering is the worse defect because sharp flakes of glaze detach from the pot and the result is dangerous on table and kitchen ware.
**Source:** Frank and Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 3rd ed., p. 89 (OCR text via StudyLib result already opened in session)
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web OCR source review.
**Notes:** This deserves emphasis because potters sometimes tolerate slight crazing, but should not tolerate shivering on functional ware.

### Rhodes’ Practical Cure for Shivering Is to Raise Glaze Expansion
**Statement:** Rhodes treats shivering as the reverse of crazing and recommends increasing the high-expansion oxides in the glaze so the glaze contracts more on cooling, reducing excessive compression.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, pp. 326-327 (Google Books snippet results for “shivering”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This is the book-backed version of the usual “reverse the crazing fix” advice.

### Thinner Glaze Layers Are Less Prone to Crack Networks
**Statement:** A recent mechanics study found that when the glaze layer is thinner than the ceramic blank, thinner glaze is less prone to cracking during cooling; it also found that glaze cracks do not form when the glaze expansion coefficient is lower than that of the blank.
**Source:** Tiantian Chen et al., “Origin and Evolution of Cracks in the Glaze Surface of a Ceramic during the Cooling Process,” *Materials* 16(16), 2023, PMC10456388, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10456388/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct PMC article review.
**Notes:** This is an academic reinforcement of two studio rules: don’t over-apply a glaze that is already prone to craze, and shivering/crazing fundamentally follow expansion direction.

### Incomplete Carbon Burnout Is a Shared Root Cause of Bloating, Black Coring, Pinholing, and Blistering
**Statement:** Steve Davis’ oxidized-bisque firing notes for Aardvark Clay state that incomplete carbon burnout is a common root cause of bloating, black coring, pinholing, blistering, and poor color development in later glaze firings.
**Source:** Steve Davis, “An Oxidized Bisque Firing,” Aardvark Clay PDF snippet via DuckDuckGo search, https://aardvarkclay.com/pdf/technical/Bisquefiring.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippet.
**Notes:** This is especially important for dense bodies, fast bisques, and kilns with poor early oxidation.

### Rough or Open Body Surfaces Promote Pinholing
**Statement:** Digitalfire notes that pinholes often occur when the clay body surface is rough, sandy, grogged, or mechanically opened by trimming, because the glaze dries over surface voids and later struggles to heal them during firing.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Glaze Pinholes, Pitting,” https://www.digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+pinholes%2C+pitting
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippet.
**Notes:** This is a practical reason to smooth rough trimmed areas before glazing, especially under stiff low-fire bases.

### Reduction and Raw Lead Make Lead-Glaze Blistering More Likely
**Statement:** Rhodes says blisters on lead-glazed ware can almost always be traced to reduction, and he also distinguishes fritted lead glazes from raw lead glazes by noting fritted versions are less prone to reduction-related blistering.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, pp. 231, 252, 332 (Google Books snippet results for “blistering” / “pinholing”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This is more historically relevant than contemporary for many studio potters, but it is a solid example of atmosphere-sensitive defect chemistry.

### Thick Pooled Glaze Commonly Causes Blisters on Bowl Interiors
**Statement:** Rhodes specifically notes that overly thick glaze application can produce blisters, especially where glaze pools on the inside bottom of bowls.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, p. 332 (Google Books snippet result for “blistering”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This gives a geometry-specific warning Claude did not include.

### Very Fluid Reactive Glazes Run Because Their Chemistry Is Already Over-Supplied With High-Expansion Fluxes
**Statement:** Digitalfire’s runny-glaze examples attribute extreme cone-6 running to high sodium and lithium content coupled with low silica, showing that dramatic glaze movement is not just an application error but often a chemistry choice.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Runny Ceramic Glazes,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/runny+ceramic+glazes ; Digitalfire picture note “An extremely runny glaze at cone 6,” https://digitalfire.com/picture/rweikpFFCT
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is useful because some “reactive” visual effects are inseparable from run risk unless the chemistry is redesigned.

### Running Can Be Managed by Layer Placement and Catch Glazes
**Statement:** Digitalfire recommends managing known runny glazes with technique, including catch glazes on exteriors and stable liner glazes inside so a lake of molten glaze does not build up in the bottom of the form.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Runny Ceramic Glazes,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/runny+ceramic+glazes ; Digitalfire picture note “Stop a runny glaze with another glaze!,” https://digitalfire.com/picture/eKg6mFh6Dc
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is a practical complement to recipe reformulation when the user wants to preserve a specific visual effect.

### Uneven Color Can Start in the Bucket, Not the Kiln
**Statement:** Rhodes warns that heavier glaze materials settle and, if the bucket is not stirred frequently, portions of the glaze used can be of the wrong composition; this is a direct route to color and surface inconsistency between pots or even during one glazing session.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, p. 300 (Google Books snippet result for “dry glaze”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This is a better-rooted explanation than blaming kiln variation for every color shift.

### A Dry, Dull, or Rough Surface Often Means Underfiring or Too-Thin Application
**Statement:** Technical handouts on glaze faults consistently describe a dry or dull glaze surface as a sign of underfiring, too-thin application, or a glaze whose formula is not mature enough for that firing; Vince Pitelka adds that if the kiln reached temperature but the glaze still looks dry, the formula needs more effective fluxing relative to alumina and silica.
**Source:** Vince Pitelka, “Common Glaze Faults,” PDF snippet via DuckDuckGo search, https://www.vincepitelka.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/03/Common-Glaze-Faults.pdf ; CeramicsWeb, “Firing Defects,” https://ceramicsweb.org/articles/tech_handouts/firing_glaze_defects.html
**Confidence:** Medium-High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** This keeps the fix split correctly between firing error and recipe mismatch.

### Bonus: Leaking and Leaching Are Separate Problems, but Crazing Makes Both More Serious
**Statement:** Digitalfire distinguishes watertightness from chemical durability: functional ware should have a dense body (typically under about 1% porosity for guaranteed watertightness), and crazing also increases concern because cracks plus porous clay can harbor contamination while unstable glaze chemistries can leach colorants.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Leaking of Fired Ceramics,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/leaking+of+fired+ceramics ; Digitalfire, “Glaze Crazing,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing ; Digitalfire, “Glaze Leaching Test,” https://digitalfire.com/test/glle
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct webpage review plus web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is worth treating as a separate troubleshooting branch in the final guide because users often confuse “doesn’t leak water” with “food-safe glaze chemistry.”
