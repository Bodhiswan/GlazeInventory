## Section 18: Common Glaze Defects

### Crawling
**Statement:** Crawling occurs when molten glaze pulls away from areas of the pot during firing, forming beads or islands of glaze with bare clay patches between them. The glaze essentially "crawls" into clumps rather than forming a smooth coating. Causes include:
1. **Dusty or greasy bisqueware** — oils from handling or dust prevent adhesion
2. **Excessively thick application** — thick glaze develops cracks during drying; these cracks become separation points when the glaze melts
3. **High surface tension in the melt** — some materials (zirconium, tin oxide, high zinc) increase surface tension
4. **High shrinkage during drying** — glazes with high raw clay content (especially uncalcined kaolin or ball clay) shrink excessively as they dry, cracking the raw glaze layer
5. **Under-fired bisque** — remaining organic matter repels glaze

**Fixes:**
- Clean bisqueware thoroughly before glazing (damp sponge wipe)
- Apply glaze at appropriate thickness (thinner coats)
- Calcine high-shrinkage materials (kaolin, zinc oxide) — replace a portion of raw clay with calcined clay
- Add a small amount of gum (CMC) to improve raw adhesion
- Reduce zirconium and tin oxide if possible
- Add a small amount of flux to reduce surface tension in the melt
- Ensure a complete bisque firing that burns out all organics
**Source:** Digitalfire "crawling" (https://digitalfire.com/trouble/crawling); Ceramic Arts Network "5 Glaze Defects and Expert Solutions" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Correct-Five-Common-Ceramic-Glaze-Defects); Glazy "Common Glaze Defects" (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/defects); BigCeramicStore "Glaze Problems" (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems); Wikipedia "Glaze defects" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glaze_defects).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple independent web sources confirmed. Crawling is one of the most documented glaze defects.
**Notes:** Crawling is more common in matte glazes than glossy ones, because matte glazes typically have higher alumina content (which increases surface tension and viscosity). Some potters intentionally exploit crawling as a decorative effect — certain crawl glazes are formulated specifically to create textured, beaded surfaces.

### Pinholing and Bloating
**Statement:** Pinholes are tiny holes (about pinhead size) in the fired glaze surface. They form when gas bubbles rise through the molten glaze but the glaze is too viscous or the firing too short for the surface to heal. Bloating is a more severe form where trapped gas causes the clay body itself to swell. Causes include:
1. **Incomplete burnout of organic matter or carbonates** in the clay body — gases escape during glaze melting
2. **Insufficient soak time at peak temperature** — glaze doesn't have time to smooth over
3. **Excessively fast ramp rate** through the 600-900°C range (burnout zone) — volatiles don't have time to escape before the surface seals
4. **Thick glaze application** — more material to outgas through
5. **Over-reduction in gas kilns** — carbon trapping at the surface
6. **Contaminated clay body** (plaster bits, calcium nodules that outgas)

**Fixes:**
- Slow down bisque firing, especially through 600-900°C, to allow complete burnout of organics and carbonate decomposition
- Add a soak (hold) at peak glaze temperature — 10-30 minutes allows the surface to heal
- Slow the ramp rate in the final 100°C approach to peak temperature
- Apply glaze at appropriate thickness (not too thick)
- Ensure clay body is properly prepared (wedged, free of contamination)
- For gas kilns: ensure proper ventilation during early firing stages
**Source:** Digitalfire "Glaze Pinholes, Pitting" (https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+pinholes,+pitting); Ceramic Arts Network "5 Glaze Defects" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Correct-Five-Common-Ceramic-Glaze-Defects); Glazy "Common Glaze Defects" (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/defects); BigCeramicStore (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple sources agree on causes and remedies.
**Notes:** Pinholing is one of the most frustrating defects because it can have multiple contributing causes simultaneously. The most reliable fix is usually a combination of slower bisque firing + soak at peak glaze temperature. The "drop-and-hold" technique (firing slightly past peak, then dropping temperature and soaking) is effective for some glazes.

### Crazing
**Statement:** Crazing is a network of fine cracks in the glaze surface, similar to crackle in old china. It occurs when the glaze's coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) is higher than the clay body's — as the kiln cools, the glaze contracts more than the clay, putting the glaze under tension until it cracks. Causes include:
1. **Glaze expansion mismatch** — too much sodium, potassium, or lithium in the glaze formula (these are high-expansion fluxes)
2. **Thick application** — thicker glaze has less body compression acting on it
3. **Underfired glaze** — the silica hasn't fully dissolved into the glass matrix
4. **Delayed crazing** — can appear weeks or months after firing, often triggered by moisture absorption into the body (common in earthenware)
5. **Thermal shock** — rapid cooling, especially through the quartz inversion point (~573°C)

**Fixes:**
- Increase silica (flint/quartz) in the glaze — silica lowers thermal expansion
- Increase alumina (add more clay/kaolin to the glaze recipe)
- Decrease high-expansion fluxes: reduce sodium (soda feldspar, nepheline syenite), potassium (potash feldspar), and lithium
- Substitute low-expansion fluxes: increase calcium (whiting), magnesium (talc, dolomite), zinc, or barium
- Apply glaze thinner
- Fire to full maturity — ensure the glaze reaches its intended cone
- Slow the cooling rate through quartz inversion (~573°C)
**Source:** Digitalfire "Glaze Crazing" (https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing); Soul Ceramics "Crazing in Pottery Glaze" (https://www.soulceramics.com/pages/crazing-pottery-glaze); Ceramic Arts Network "Common Glaze Faults" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/common-glaze-faults-and-how-to-correct-them); Glazy (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/defects); Wikipedia "Glaze defects" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glaze_defects).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** This is one of the best-documented glaze defects. Multiple sources agree.
**Notes:** Crazing makes functional ware (food-safe) problematic — cracks harbor bacteria and can weaken the piece over time. Some traditions (raku, Chinese Guan/Ge ware) intentionally use crazing as a decorative effect, sometimes rubbing ink or tea into the cracks to accentuate the pattern. The key point is that crazing is always a fit problem between glaze and body — the fix must address the expansion mismatch, not just the symptoms.

### Shivering
**Statement:** Shivering is the opposite of crazing — the glaze is under too much compression (its CTE is much lower than the body's). Instead of cracking like crazing, the glaze literally lifts off the body in sharp, razor-edged flakes or slivers. This is a **safety hazard** for functional ware because glass slivers can end up in food or drink. Causes:
1. **Glaze expansion too low relative to the body** — too much silica in the glaze, or a body with very high expansion
2. **Excessively thick application** — amplifies compression stress
3. **Sharp edges on the form** — compression concentrates at edges, lips, and rims

**Fixes:**
- Decrease silica in the glaze
- Increase high-expansion fluxes (sodium, potassium) — increase soda feldspar or nepheline syenite
- Decrease silica in the clay body (alternatively)
- Apply glaze thinner, especially on edges
- Round off sharp edges on forms (compression concentrates at sharp angles)
**Source:** Glazy "Common Glaze Defects" (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/defects); Ceramic Arts Network "5 Glaze Defects" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Correct-Five-Common-Ceramic-Glaze-Defects); Digitalfire "ceramic glaze defects" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/ceramic+glaze+defects); BigCeramicStore (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple sources confirmed. Shivering is well-documented as the inverse of crazing.
**Notes:** A small amount of compression in the glaze is actually desirable — it makes the glaze stronger and more durable. The problem is when compression exceeds the glaze's strength. Shivering is less common than crazing but more dangerous. It's most common on rims and edges where the body cross-section is thin.

### Running and Dripping
**Statement:** Running occurs when glaze becomes too fluid during firing and flows down vertical surfaces, pooling at the base and potentially fusing the piece to the kiln shelf. Causes:
1. **Overfiring** — firing beyond the glaze's intended cone makes it too fluid
2. **Excessively thick application** — more material + gravity = more flow
3. **High flux content** — too much flux in the recipe creates an overly fluid melt
4. **Layered glazes** — two glazes can create a eutectic (lower melting point at their interface), causing excessive flow even at the correct temperature
5. **Kiln atmosphere issues** — reduction can increase fluidity of some glazes

**Fixes:**
- Fire to the correct cone (don't overfire)
- Apply glaze thinner, especially on the lower half of vertical pieces
- Reduce flux in the recipe (replace some flux with alumina or silica)
- Test layered combinations on vertical test tiles before committing to a piece
- Keep the bottom 1/4 inch of the pot glaze-free as a safety margin
- Use a catch tray (a bisque dish or kiln shelf cookie under the piece) for glazes known to run
**Source:** Digitalfire "ceramic glaze defects" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/ceramic+glaze+defects); BigCeramicStore (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems); Ceramic Arts Network (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/common-glaze-faults-and-how-to-correct-them).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple sources confirmed.
**Notes:** Running is especially common with combination/layered glazes — the eutectic effect can make glazes that are perfectly stable alone become very runny when layered. This is why testing combinations on vertical test tiles is so important. Many Cone 6 potters routinely leave the bottom 1/4" unglazed and use kiln cookies as insurance.

### Color Inconsistency
**Statement:** Inconsistent color across a piece or between firings can have multiple causes:
1. **Uneven glaze thickness** — thicker areas appear darker/more saturated; thinner areas appear lighter
2. **Contamination** — cross-contamination between glazes (especially copper, which is notorious for "traveling" in the kiln)
3. **Kiln atmosphere variation** — different locations in the kiln experience different amounts of heat, airflow, or reduction
4. **Inconsistent mixing** — heavy colorant particles (iron, cobalt, manganese) settle and must be thoroughly stirred before each use
5. **Batch variation** — different batches of raw materials can have slightly different chemistry

**Fixes:**
- Apply glaze at consistent thickness (use SG measurement)
- Stir/remix glaze thoroughly before each use
- Sieve glaze regularly to ensure even distribution of colorants
- Clean glaze application tools between colors
- Be aware of kiln placement effects — document where pieces are placed and correlate with results
- For critical color matching, use glaze from the same batch
**Source:** Digitalfire "ceramic glaze defects" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/ceramic+glaze+defects); BigCeramicStore (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems); general ceramic practice.
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Training data + web sources.
**Notes:** Copper contamination is the most dramatic version of this — even tiny amounts of copper can create green spots or blushes on other glazes. In shared studios, pots with copper glazes should be fired separately or placed where drips won't reach other work. Manganese and chrome are also notorious contaminators.

### Blistering
**Statement:** Blisters are large bubbles (larger than pinholes) that form in the glaze surface, often leaving a "moonscape" of burst craters. They're caused by gas generation at or near peak temperature when the glaze is already sealed and viscous. Causes:
1. **Excessively thick application** — more material = more gas trapped
2. **Volatile flux components** — sodium oxide and borate frits become volatile above ~1200°C
3. **Body outgassing** — gases from bone ash, calcium carbonate, or other materials escaping through sealed glaze
4. **Residual moisture** — pieces not fully dry before glaze firing
5. **Overfiring** — can cause flux volatilization
6. **Excessively fast firing** — gases released too quickly

**Fixes:**
- Apply glaze thinner
- Ensure pieces are completely dry before loading the kiln
- Slow the firing, especially in the upper temperature range
- Add a soak at peak temperature to allow bubbles to heal
- Reduce volatile materials in the glaze recipe
- Ensure proper bisque firing to eliminate body outgassing sources
**Source:** Digitalfire "Glaze Blisters" (https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+blisters); Ceramic Arts Network "5 Glaze Defects" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Correct-Five-Common-Ceramic-Glaze-Defects); Glazy (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/defects).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple sources confirmed.
**Notes:** Blistering vs. pinholing is a matter of degree — same mechanism (gas through molten glaze), different scale. Some potters find that switching to a slower cooling rate helps more than a soak at peak temperature, because it gives the glaze more time in its fluid state to heal.

### Dry / Underfired Glaze
**Statement:** A dry, rough, or matte-looking glaze that should be glossy indicates underfiring — the glaze hasn't reached its full melting temperature. The glaze particles have sintered (stuck together) but not fully fused into a smooth glass. Causes:
1. **Kiln didn't reach target temperature** — element degradation in electric kilns, insufficient gas pressure in gas kilns
2. **Cone placement error** — firing to the wrong cone
3. **Glaze applied to the wrong cone range** — e.g., a Cone 10 glaze fired to Cone 6
4. **Cold spots in the kiln** — uneven heat distribution (bottom of kiln, near doors)
5. **Glaze recipe error** — insufficient flux

**Fixes:**
- Verify kiln temperature with witness cones (not just the controller)
- Check kiln elements (electric) or burner efficiency (gas) regularly
- Ensure glaze is formulated for your firing temperature
- Adjust kiln loading for even heat distribution
- Refire to the correct temperature (often successful — underfired glazes usually fire perfectly on a second pass)
**Source:** BigCeramicStore (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems); Digitalfire (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/ceramic+glaze+defects); Ceramic Arts Network (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/common-glaze-faults-and-how-to-correct-them).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple sources confirmed.
**Notes:** Refiring is often the simplest fix — most underfired glazes fire perfectly on a second attempt at the correct temperature. However, refiring can change the glaze character slightly (some glazes develop different colors or surfaces when refired). Electric kiln elements degrade over time and should be tested regularly — a kiln that used to easily reach Cone 6 may struggle after hundreds of firings.
