# Arbitration Log

When Claude, GPT, and Grok produce conflicting claims, they are logged here for Bodhi to decide.

## Rules
1. Source wins over memory (specific citation > training recall)
2. Multiple agreeing sources > single source
3. Manufacturer data is authoritative for their own products
4. Academic/museum sources are authoritative for history
5. Practitioner sources are authoritative for technique

## Phase A: Foundations + Troubleshooting

### Crazing: process tweaks vs. actual cure
- **Claude wrote:** Slowing cooling through quartz inversion and applying glaze thinner are listed as “fixes” for crazing.
- **GPT concern:** Hamer, Rhodes, and especially Digitalfire all frame crazing primarily as a glaze-fit / thermal-expansion mismatch. Process tweaks can delay or slightly reduce visible crazing, but they do not solve bad fit if chemistry remains wrong.
- **Suggested ruling:** Keep process notes as secondary mitigations, but state clearly that the primary cure is changing body/glaze fit chemistry.
- **Sources:** Frank & Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, p. 89; Tony Hansen, “Glaze Crazing,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing
- **Ruling:** AGREED — chemistry fit is the primary cure. Process tweaks are secondary mitigations only.

### Dampening bisque: helpful vs. harmful
- **Claude wrote:** Dampening bisque before glazing is broadly useful when absorption is too fast.
- **GPT concern:** Linda Arbuckle’s majolica notes support slight dampening for pickup and dust control, but also warn that excess water can move the raw glaze and contribute to crawling.
- **Suggested ruling:** Keep dampening as a conditional technique, but explicitly warn against over-wetting and note that dense or already-slow-absorbing bisque may not benefit.
- **Sources:** Linda Arbuckle, “Five Great Ceramic Glazing Techniques,” pp. 4-5, https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/docs/default-source/uploadedfiles/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/fg17-5-glazingtechniques.pdf
- **Ruling:** AGREED — keep dampening as conditional technique with explicit warnings about over-wetting and dense bisque.

### Bisque temp for pinhole prevention: “hotter bisque” wording
- **Grok/Mayco wrote:** Bisque should be fired “1-2 cones hotter than glaze fire” to prevent pinholes.
- **Claude/standard practice:** Bisque is typically fired *lower* than glaze fire (e.g., bisque cone 04, glaze cone 6). The point is that bisque should be hot enough to burn out organics completely.
- **Analysis:** These aren't contradictory — Mayco is discussing low-fire work where bisque at cone 04 is indeed hotter than glaze at cone 06. The wording “hotter than glaze fire” is specific to low-fire context. For mid/high-fire, bisque is almost always lower than glaze fire.
- **Suggested ruling:** Clarify in the guide that bisque temperature should be high enough for complete organic burnout. For low-fire (cone 06 glaze), this means bisque to cone 04. For mid/high-fire (cone 6+ glaze), bisque to cone 04-06 is standard.
- **Sources:** Mayco Ceramics 101 p. 12; Digitalfire “bisque” glossary

### Hydrometer usefulness: dismissive vs. qualified
- **Claude wrote:** Hydrometers are “problematic” for ceramic glazes and weight-per-volume is “preferred.”
- **GPT wrote:** Hydrometers “are workable, but less informative for narrow ceramic ranges and thixotropic slurries” — qualifying rather than dismissing.
- **Suggested ruling:** Use GPT's more balanced framing. Hydrometers work but have limitations in the ceramic SG range. Weight-per-volume is more reliable for most potters.
- **Sources:** Digitalfire “specific gravity”; Sue McLeod Ceramics; The Studio Manager
