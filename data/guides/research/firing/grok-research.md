### [Oxidation vs Reduction / Atmosphere Effects on Commercial Glazes]
**Statement:** Most commercial brush-on glazes, including lines such as Mayco Stoneware, Mayco Elements, and AMACO Potter's Choice, are formulated and tested for oxidation firing. Reduction alters color, fluidity, and surface dramatically and makes label predictions unreliable for iron-, copper-, chrome-, rutile-, or celadon-bearing glazes.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; Digitalfire, "Oxidation Firing," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/oxidation+firing
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Most relevant at cone 5-6 and cone 10. Oxidation gives brighter, more predictable colors; reduction can darken iron, turn copper red, and mute or alter chrome/rutile effects.

### [Oxidation vs Reduction / Specific Oxide Behavior Changes In Reduction]
**Statement:** In reduction, iron oxide shifts toward FeO and becomes a stronger flux, producing fluid celadons and tenmokus rather than simple brown iron response; copper can yield reds and lusters instead of greens; chrome and rutile responses shift toward more muted or speckled effects; classic celadon depends on iron reduction for its blue-green color.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Oxidation Firing" and related reduction entries, https://digitalfire.com/glossary/oxidation+firing
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with cross-checking from general ceramics references
**Notes:** Broadly applies from cone 5 upward. Low-fire commercial glazes are usually not designed to benefit from reduction and may become unstable.

### [Commercial Glazes / Label Chips Stop Being Predictive In Reduction]
**Statement:** Manufacturer color charts and cone labels for commercial glazes are based on oxidation firings; in reduction, gas, wood, soda, or salt conditions the same glaze can change color, run more, or develop unintended textures, so direct testing is required.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Mayco explicitly distinguishes cone 6 oxidation chips from cone 10 reduction chips in this guide.

### [Kiln Types / Electric Kilns Provide The Most Stable Oxidation For Commercial Glazes]
**Statement:** Electric kilns naturally maintain oxidation and generally deliver the most repeatable, label-faithful results for commercial brush-on glazes; gas, wood, soda, and salt kilns introduce atmosphere shifts or atmospheric deposition that change both color and surface.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; Digitalfire kiln-atmosphere references
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with technical cross-checking
**Notes:** Especially relevant for cone 5-6 commercial users comparing results to jar labels or manufacturer sample tiles.

### [Atmospheric Kilns / Soda, Salt, And Wood Add Surface Material During Firing]
**Statement:** Soda, salt, and wood firings do not just heat the glaze already on the pot. They also deposit sodium, potassium, ash, and carbon-bearing materials that flux the surface further and create effects such as flashing and orange-peel textures, reducing repeatability compared with electric kilns.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; Digitalfire atmosphere references
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Most relevant at cone 5-10. Useful as a caution for anyone expecting manufacturer oxidation chips to translate directly into atmospheric firings.

### [Cones / Pyrometric Cones Measure Heatwork Rather Than A Single Temperature]
**Statement:** Pyrometric cones measure cumulative heatwork - the combined effect of time and temperature - by controlled bending of ceramic materials. A cone 6 does not bend at one fixed temperature but over a range that changes with firing rate.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Pyrometric Cone," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/pyrometric+cone ; Orton cone guides
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is why controller readouts and witness-cone results can disagree even when the kiln appears to reach the target number.

### [Witness Cones / Manufacturers Still Require Them For Commercial Glazes]
**Statement:** Mayco and AMACO both direct users to use witness cones in every firing because kiln sitters and controllers can misread due to element wear, loading, or ramp differences; for a properly fired cone, the tip should bend to approximately level with the base.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; AMACO product guidance and Orton cone reading conventions
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Particularly important for matte and reactive glazes, which are visually sensitive to small under- or over-fire differences.

### [Ramp Rate / Slow Early Ramps Reduce Defects In Commercial Ware]
**Statement:** Slower early ramps in bisque and glaze firings allow organics and chemically combined water to burn out more completely, reducing pinholes, blisters, and carbon-coring problems in commercial brush-on glaze systems.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "C6DHSC" schedule, https://digitalfire.com/schedule/c6dhsc
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Most useful for thicker applications, dense bodies, and low-fire or mid-fire commercial ware with defect-prone clear or reactive glazes.

### [Soaks / Short Holds Can Heal Pinholes But Long Peak Holds Can Overfire Glazes]
**Statement:** A short 15-30 minute hold at or just below peak temperature can help a glaze heal pinholes and blisters, but prolonged holds at peak can also increase running, color bleed, or over-maturity in commercial glazes.
**Source:** Digitalfire schedule notes for C6DHSC and PLC6DS, https://digitalfire.com/schedule/c6dhsc ; https://www.digitalfire.com/schedule/117
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Most relevant at cone 5-6. Useful for glossy and reactive glazes, but not universally beneficial.

### [Cooling Rate / Slow Cooling Promotes Crystal Growth And Matte Development]
**Statement:** Controlled slow cooling, for example around 150F/hr from roughly 2100F toward 1400F, promotes micro-crystal growth and therefore matte development, variegation, and rutile-blue effects in cone-6 glaze systems.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "C6DHSC" schedule, https://digitalfire.com/schedule/c6dhsc ; Digitalfire, "Rutile Blue Glazes," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/rutile+blue+glazes
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Especially relevant for electric cone-6 oxidation work. Fast cooling preserves gloss but can reduce these visual effects.

### [Cooling Rate / Fast Cooling Can Preserve Gloss And Prevent Over-Running]
**Statement:** Faster cooling after maturity can keep some commercial glazes glossier and limit over-running or unwanted crystal development, even though it may also reduce matte or crystal effects users like in other lines.
**Source:** AMACO product guidance for glaze lines that react differently to slow cooling, cross-checked with Digitalfire cooling discussions
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Web search and cross-source synthesis
**Notes:** This is manufacturer-specific and should not be generalized across all commercial lines.

### [Controlled Schedules / Digitalfire C6DHSC Is A Strong Cone-6 Reactive-Glaze Starting Schedule]
**Statement:** Digitalfire's C6DHSC schedule - slow early ramp, brief peak hold, drop-and-hold, then controlled slow cool - is a strong starting schedule for many reactive cone-6 glazes because it supports burnout, heals pinholes, and improves variegation and crystal growth.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "C6DHSC," https://digitalfire.com/schedule/c6dhsc
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Best treated as a starting point rather than a universal standard. Product-specific manufacturer guidance still takes priority.

### [Kiln Loading / Shelf Position And Density Affect Maturity]
**Statement:** Top and bottom shelves, ware near elements, and densely packed shelves can all mature differently from center positions in an electric kiln, producing color, gloss, and fit inconsistency in commercial glazes; witness cones should be placed on multiple shelves to reveal this.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; Digitalfire heatwork references
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with technical cross-checking
**Notes:** Especially important when comparing test tiles or troubleshooting one shelf that always looks different.

### [Venting / Early Venting And Proper Burnout Reduce Later Pinholes And Blisters]
**Statement:** Proper early venting and complete bisque burnout dramatically reduce pinholes and blisters in later low-fire and cone-5/6 commercial glaze firings by removing organics and carbonates before the glaze seals over the body.
**Source:** Digitalfire schedule rationale and Mayco low-fire guidance stressing oxygen circulation
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with cross-checking
**Notes:** Especially important for low-fire clears and thick brush-on applications.

### [Refiring / It Can Heal Minor Underfire Or Pinholes But Carries Real Risks]
**Statement:** A refire to the original cone can sometimes heal minor pinholes or under-matured commercial glazes, but repeated or poorly judged refires often increase running, devitrification, color loss, or glaze-fit problems.
**Source:** Digitalfire refiring references and general commercial-glaze troubleshooting guidance
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with secondary web cross-checking
**Notes:** Best treated as a test-first repair strategy, not a default solution.

### [Commercial Glaze Caveats / Label Cone, Actual Maturity, And Preferred Look Are Not The Same]
**Statement:** A commercial glaze's labeled cone is a firing range, not a guarantee of the single best appearance. Some cone-6 glazes look better at cone 5, some at cone 6, and some at cone 6 with controlled cooling depending on the product line.
**Source:** Mayco, "Using Mayco Stoneware Glazes" (2023 PDF), https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/UsingMaycoStonewareGlazes-2023.pdf ; manufacturer product pages
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Strongly relevant for commercial users comparing matte, rutile, crystal, and reduction-look lines across different kilns and schedules.

### [Bonus / Crystalline And Luster Firings Need Their Own Specialized Schedules]
**Statement:** Crystalline glazes and low-fire luster or overglaze firings operate in separate scheduling logic from standard glaze firings: crystalline glazes depend on controlled crystal-growth windows, while lusters and overglazes fire much lower, typically in strong oxidation at approximately cone 018-020.
**Source:** Digitalfire crystalline schedule discussions ; Orton low-fire cone information
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading with Orton cross-checking
**Notes:** Useful bonus context, but secondary to the main commercial brush-on oxidation audience.
