### [Cone & Heatwork / A Cone Measures Heatwork, Not Just Peak Temperature]
**Statement:** A pyrometric cone measures the combined effect of time and temperature on ware, so a glaze or clay can be underfired at the "right" peak temperature if the firing is too fast and overfired at a lower peak if the heatwork is extended.
**Source:** Orton Ceramic, "PYROMETRIC CONES FAQ," https://www.ortonceramic.com/pyrometric-cones-faq
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is the core reason witness cones remain more trustworthy than controller temperature alone when judging glaze maturity.

### [Cone & Heatwork / Cone 6 Does Not Equal One Fixed Temperature]
**Statement:** Orton's self-supporting cone 6 bends at different endpoint temperatures depending on heating rate: about 1185°C at a slow 15°C/hr rate, 1222°C at a medium 60°C/hr rate, and 1243°C at a fast 150°C/hr rate.
**Source:** Orton Ceramic, "Cone #6 SELF-SUPPORTING (25/BOX)," https://www.ortonceramic.com/product-page/6-self-supporting-25-box
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is one of the clearest manufacturer-backed explanations for why two kilns can both say "cone 6" but produce different glaze results.

### [Witness Cones / Controllers Still Need Cone Verification]
**Statement:** Electronic controllers infer kiln temperature from a thermocouple signal, and thermocouples shift with repeated heating and cooling, so witness cones are still needed to verify that the kiln delivered the intended heatwork.
**Source:** Orton Ceramic, "Are Pyrometric Cones Necessary in an Electronically Controlled Kiln?," https://www.ortonceramic.com/post/are-pyrometric-cones-necessary-in-an-electronically-controlled-kiln
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This matters even more for commercial glazes, because product-chip expectations are usually tied to properly delivered cone work, not simply a displayed controller number.

### [Witness Cones / Placement And Reading Affect Accuracy]
**Statement:** Witness cones should be placed on shelves where they can be seen clearly, kept out of drafts, and positioned about 3 inches from the elements; Mayco recommends using guide, firing, and guard cones, while Orton considers a cone properly fired when the tip reaches roughly the 5-to-6-o'clock position.
**Source:** Mayco, "Ceramics 101," pp. 8-9, https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf ; Orton Ceramic, "PYROMETRIC CONES FAQ," https://www.ortonceramic.com/pyrometric-cones-faq
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Poor cone placement can make a kiln look less uniform than it really is, or hide a hot/cool shelf that is affecting glaze color and maturity.

### [Commercial Glazes / Most Brush-On Midfire Systems Assume Oxidation And Medium-Speed Firings]
**Statement:** AMACO's midfire commercial glaze pages repeatedly note that pictured results were fired in oxidation and that their temperature equivalents are based on normal medium-speed firings, meaning reduction firings and unusual schedules can make label chips much less predictive.
**Source:** AMACO, "(C) Celadon," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/c-celadon/ ; AMACO, "(SM) Satin Matte," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/sm-satin-matte/ ; AMACO, "(HF) High Fire," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/hf-high-fire/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is especially relevant for Glaze Library's commercial audience, because many users treat a jar label as universal when it is really tied to a firing context.

### [Oxidation vs Reduction / Some Commercial Glazes Simulate Reduction Looks In Oxidation, But They Are Still Schedule-Sensitive]
**Statement:** Some commercial lines are intentionally formulated to mimic reduction surfaces in electric oxidation, but the effect is still dependent on application thickness, cone, and clay body rather than being a true one-to-one substitute for reduction firing.
**Source:** AMACO, "Potter's Choice Advantage," p. 2, https://amaco.com/asset/662032abdf5bd/Potters%20Choice%20Advantage_03-07-24_COPIER.pdf ; AMACO, "(SH) Shino," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/sh-shino/ ; AMACO, "PC-70 Copper Red: Tips and Tricks," https://amaco.com/resources/blog/pc-70-copper-red-tips-and-tricks
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Useful examples are AMACO's Potter's Choice and Shino lines, plus PC-70 Copper Red, which creates a self-reducing red effect inside the glaze without a reduction kiln.

### [Oxidation vs Reduction / Atmosphere Changes Metal Oxidation State And Therefore Color]
**Statement:** Reduction firing alters glaze color because carbon monoxide and soot remove oxygen from metallic oxides in the glaze; copper shifts from green Cu2+ behavior toward red Cu1+, while iron reduction to Fe2+ drives celadon/chun blues and classic rutile-blue response.
**Source:** Ceramic Arts Network, "Techno File: Reduction Misnomer," https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/ceramics-monthly/ceramics-monthly-article/Techno-File-Reduction-Misnomer
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is why commercial oxidation chips become unreliable in gas reduction, especially for iron, copper, rutile, and celadon-style glazes.

### [Kiln Types / Electric Kilns Are Usually More Predictable For Commercial Glaze Users]
**Statement:** Electric kilns are generally the most practical and repeatable choice for commercial glaze users because they avoid ash deposition and aggressive atmosphere shifts, while fuel kilns introduce more variables in oxidation level, flame path, and surface development.
**Source:** Mayco, "Ceramics 101," p. 8, https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf ; Coyote Clay & Color, "Cone 6 Glazes," https://coyoteclay.com/Tiles.html
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Mayco explicitly describes electric kilns as the most commonly used kiln and calls gas/oil less practical for whiteware-style glaze work; Coyote states its premium glazes are designed for the electric kiln.

### [Wood, Salt, And Soda / Atmospheric Kilns Add Material To The Surface, Not Just Heat]
**Statement:** Wood, salt, and soda firings do more than heat the glaze recipe already on the pot: wood ash can volatilize and flux the surface, while salt and soda vapors react with silica and alumina on the ware to create new sodium-bearing surface glaze effects.
**Source:** Ceramic Arts Network, "An Introduction to Fuel-Burning Kilns," https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/An-Introduction-to-Fuel-Burning-Kilns ; Ceramic Arts Network, "Techno File: Determining Glaze Color," https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/ceramics-monthly/ceramics-monthly-article/techno-file--determining-glaze-color
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is a major reason atmospheric firings are less chip-predictable than electric commercial-glaze firings. Salt and soda can also darken or gloss nearby glaze surfaces because sodium acts as a flux.

### [Venting & Burnout / Slow, Oxygen-Rich Bisque Firing Prevents Many Later Glaze Defects]
**Statement:** Incomplete burnout of carbon and sulfur during bisque firing can later show up as black coring, bloating, pinholing, blistering, and poor color development in the glaze firing; Steve Davis specifically recommends strong ventilation and slow progress through 1292-1652°F (700-900°C), where inorganic carbon and sulfur oxidize.
**Source:** Ceramic Arts Network, "A Bisque Firing Schedule to Help Prevent Glaze Faults," https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/A-Bisque-Firing-Schedule-to-Help-Prevent-Glaze-Faults
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Thick ware, dense loads, and high-iron clay bodies need more patience here than thin, open loads of white stoneware or porcelain.

### [Venting & Burnout / Downdraft Venting Improves Oxidation, Uniformity, And Color Reliability]
**Statement:** Skutt states that downdraft venting removes fumes directly from the kiln, improves chamber heat distribution, promotes more complete oxidation, and can cut top-to-bottom temperature differences to about half normal; its EnviroVent 2 is rated at 140 CFM.
**Source:** Skutt, "The Facts About Kiln Ventilation," https://skutt.com/pdf/envirovent2/EV2_Flyer.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Skutt also specifically connects poor venting to black core, pinholes, blisters, dull/cloudy color, and weak red-orange-yellow glaze color. This is especially relevant in electric kilns loaded with commercial low-fire colors and clears.

### [Ramp Rate / Fast Firings Commonly Under-Develop Standard Glazes]
**Statement:** Standard ceramic glazes usually need moderate firing rates to mature properly; Mayco recommends roughly 250-350°F per hour and warns against exceeding 500°F per hour, while Skutt notes that Fast mode is mainly intended for low-temperature china paints, lusters, overglazes, and decals rather than most bisque or glaze firings.
**Source:** Mayco, "Ceramics 101," p. 8, https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf ; Skutt, "Best Practices for Loading and Firing Your Skutt Kiln," https://skutt.com/best-practices-for-loading-and-firing-your-skutt-kiln/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Under-mature fast firings often show up as chalky or muted surfaces, weak color, or immature body/glaze fit rather than dramatic visible defects.

### [Soaks & Drop-And-Hold / A Lower Hold Often Heals Bubbles Better Than A Long Peak Soak]
**Statement:** Digitalfire's drop-and-soak guidance is to approach maturity slowly, hold only briefly at peak, then drop 100-200°F and hold there, because bubbles often keep percolating during a top-temperature soak but can burst and heal when the cooler melt becomes slightly more viscous.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Drop-and-Soak Firing," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/drop-and-soak%20firing ; Digitalfire, "PLC6DS Firing Schedule," https://www.digitalfire.com/schedule/117
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is especially useful for pinholing and blistering in electric kilns. It is not a universal cure, and very fluid glazes may need a larger drop or thinner application as well.

### [Controlled Schedules / Example Commercial Cone-5-6 Schedule From Coyote]
**Statement:** Coyote's published slow-cool starting schedule for cone-5/6 glazes is: 100°F/hr to 220°F, 350°F/hr to 2000°F, 150°F/hr to 2200°F with a 15-minute hold, 500°F/hr down to 2150°F with a 15-minute hold, then 125°F/hr to 1400°F.
**Source:** Coyote Clay & Color, "Glazing and Firing Instructions," https://www.coyoteclay.com/Instructions.html
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Coyote also explicitly says this is only a starting point and must be tuned to the kiln and load. It is valuable because few commercial glaze makers publish a full slow-cool schedule this plainly.

### [Controlled Cooling / Slow Cooling Promotes Crystal Growth, Mattes, And Reactive Surfaces]
**Statement:** Slow cooling extends the temperature window in which crystals form, so it often increases visible crystallization or produces microcrystalline matte surfaces; Coyote says this directly, and AMACO's Cosmos line says controlled cooling increases crystal growth even though the glaze can also be fired successfully on a normal medium-speed schedule.
**Source:** Coyote Clay & Color, "Glazing and Firing Instructions," https://www.coyoteclay.com/Instructions.html ; AMACO, "(CO) Cosmos," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/co-cosmos/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Beneficial slow-cool response is glaze-specific. It often helps crystal and reactive midrange glazes, but it can make already-fluid or crystal-prone glazes move more than expected.

### [Cooling Rate / Slow Cooling Can Also Cloud Clears And Over-Matte Some Surfaces]
**Statement:** Slow cooling can hurt transparency by allowing boron-bearing crystals and microstructure to grow in clears; Digitalfire documents low-fire and cone-6 cases where the same glaze clouded in a slow-cooling kiln but stayed clearer in a faster-cooling one.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Clouding in Ceramic Glazes," https://digitalfire.com/trouble/clouding%20in%20ceramic%20glazes
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is an important counterweight to blanket "always slow cool" advice. Commercial clears, especially boron-rich clears, may need faster cooling than variegated mattes or crystalline surfaces.

### [Kiln Loading / Top, Bottom, Center, And Thermocouple Clearance Change Results]
**Statement:** Loading pattern materially affects glaze maturity: Skutt says the top and bottom shelves are most prone to underfiring if stacked too tightly, the center of the kiln is generally hottest, the top shelf should sit about 5 inches below the lid, the first shelf should be 1 inch off the floor, and there should be about 2 inches of clearance around the thermocouple tip.
**Source:** Skutt, "Best Practices for Loading and Firing Your Skutt Kiln," https://skutt.com/best-practices-for-loading-and-firing-your-skutt-kiln/ ; Skutt, "Appendix 4 - Loading Tips," https://skutt.com/pdf/op_manual/2011_op_manual/KM_Manual-11_Appendix4.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** The same Skutt guidance also recommends about 1 inch of clearance between shelf edge and kiln wall for heat flow. This helps explain section-by-section color inconsistency in commercial glazes that otherwise seemed "applied correctly."

### [Kiln Loading / Uneven Or Dense Loads Distort Heatwork]
**Statement:** Very uneven or densely packed loads can make a digitally controlled kiln fire inaccurately relative to ware position, because radiant heat cannot penetrate tightly stacked shelves evenly even if the controller sees the target temperature near the thermocouple.
**Source:** Skutt, "Best Practices for Loading and Firing Your Skutt Kiln," https://skutt.com/best-practices-for-loading-and-firing-your-skutt-kiln/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Skutt advises spreading partial loads throughout the chamber if the kiln is around 40% full, but concentrating them on one or two center shelves if the load is much smaller. This is a practical, high-value detail for test firing and commercial glaze comparison work.

### [Refiring / Useful For Some Repairs And Decorative Firings, But Risky For Fully Matured Glaze Ware]
**Statement:** Refiring is normal for some processes such as decals, lusters, or successive firings, but defect-repair refires carry real risks because the already-fired ceramic matrix is now denser and more brittle, while the glaze can remelt earlier and behave more fluidly than it did on the first firing.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Refiring Ceramics," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/refiring%2Bceramics
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** Digitalfire specifically warns about dunting, blistering, bloating, and body-color changes on refire. The risks rise on vitreous bodies and highly fluid, reactive glazes.

### [Refiring / Many Reactive And Self-Reducing Glazes Do Not Recover Well On A Second Firing]
**Statement:** Refiring often worsens reactive glaze problems rather than healing them: Digitalfire notes that rutile-blue and blister-prone glazes commonly run and blister more on refire, and AMACO explicitly says adding more glaze and refiring will not rescue PC-70 Copper Red because its reduction materials have already burned off.
**Source:** Digitalfire, "Refiring Ceramics," https://digitalfire.com/glossary/refiring%2Bceramics ; AMACO, "PC-70 Copper Red: Tips and Tricks," https://amaco.com/resources/blog/pc-70-copper-red-tips-and-tricks
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is a high-value caveat for commercial users, because "just refire it" is often repeated too casually in pottery communities.

### [Commercial Glaze Caveats / Label Cone And Best-Looking Cone Are Not Always The Same]
**Statement:** A glaze's rated firing range is not always the same as its visually best result: AMACO says PC-70 Copper Red is reddest at cone 5 and turns more purple-red with more running risk at cone 6, while Mayco says SW-135 Wintergreen is a true matte at cone 5 but becomes satin at cone 6.
**Source:** AMACO, "PC-70 Copper Red: Tips and Tricks," https://amaco.com/resources/blog/pc-70-copper-red-tips-and-tricks ; Mayco, "SW-135 Wintergreen," https://www.maycocolors.com/product/sw-135-wintergreen/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is one of the most important real-world commercial-glaze lessons: maturity range, marketing cone, and preferred look are related but not identical.

### [Commercial Glaze Caveats / Some Products Reward Small Schedule Adjustments, Others Explicitly Do Not Need Them]
**Statement:** Manufacturer guidance for commercial glazes is product-specific rather than universal: AMACO says Cosmos can be fired on a normal medium-speed schedule but gains more crystal growth with controlled cooling, while Mayco states its Stoneware Crystal glazes do not require any particular firing schedule to create their basic crystal effects.
**Source:** AMACO, "(CO) Cosmos," https://shop.amaco.com/glazes-underglazes/high-fire-glazes/co-cosmos/ ; Mayco, "Stoneware," https://www.maycocolors.com/color/fired/stoneware/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct web source reading
**Notes:** This is a useful corrective to overly generic advice about always slow cooling or always adding holds. The right schedule depends on the chemistry and the effect the line was engineered to produce.
