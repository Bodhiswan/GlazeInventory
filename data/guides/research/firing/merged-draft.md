## 15. Atmosphere and Kiln Type

For Glaze Library's audience, the most important starting point is simple: commercial brush-on glazes are usually tested and photographed in oxidation, most often in electric kilns. Mayco's stoneware guide and multiple AMACO product pages make this explicit by tying sample results to oxidation and to normal medium-speed firings. That means label chips are not universal truth. They are results from a specific firing context.

Reduction changes that context immediately. The chemistry reason is that reduction removes oxygen from metallic oxides in the glaze, shifting both color and melt behavior. Copper can move from green oxidation response toward red reduction response; iron becomes more flux-active and drives celadon, chun, and tenmoku behavior; rutile and chrome-bearing effects also shift noticeably. For a commercial-glaze user, the practical takeaway is straightforward: once you leave oxidation, the jar label becomes a rough suggestion rather than a prediction.

Electric kilns are therefore the most stable and repeatable firing environment for commercial glazes. They do not add ash or sodium vapor, and they usually maintain oxidation more consistently than fuel kilns. Gas, wood, soda, and salt kilns all introduce extra variables in flame path, atmosphere, and deposition. Wood ash can flux the surface. Soda and salt vapors can form their own sodium-rich glaze effects and alter nearby glaze color and gloss. In other words, atmospheric kilns do not just heat the glaze recipe on the pot; they partly rewrite the surface chemistry during the firing itself.

One useful middle ground is the commercial "reduction-look in oxidation" glaze. AMACO explicitly markets Potter's Choice and related lines this way, and products like PC-70 Copper Red are formulated to create a reduction-like effect inside the glaze even in an electric kiln. That can be powerful for commercial users, but it still depends on thickness, cone, and clay body. It is not the same as a truly reduction-fired glaze.

## 16. Heatwork, Schedules, and Cooling

The single most important firing idea for potters to understand is that a cone measures heatwork, not just temperature. Orton's own data shows that cone 6 bends at different temperatures depending on heating rate; there is no one fixed "cone 6 temperature." That is why two kilns can both report cone 6 and still produce different glaze results. It is also why witness cones matter even in digitally controlled kilns. Controllers read thermocouples, and thermocouples drift with use. Witness cones reveal what the ware actually experienced.

The next major issue is burnout before sealing. Many glaze defects blamed on the glaze itself actually begin earlier, when the body has not burned out carbon, sulfur, and other volatiles completely. Steve Davis and other technical sources point to the 700-900C range as critical for oxidation of those materials; poor burnout here contributes to black coring, pinholing, blistering, and bloating later. Good venting matters for the same reason. Skutt's venting guidance ties poor venting not just to fumes in the room but to black core, pinholes, cloudy colors, weak warm-color development, and uneven firing results inside the kiln.

At glaze temperature, the most useful practical distinction is between a long peak soak and a drop-and-hold. Digitalfire argues that a long top soak can keep bubbles active in a very fluid melt, while a short peak hold followed by a drop of roughly 100-200F can let bubbles burst and heal more effectively in a slightly stiffer glaze. This is excellent defect-control logic, especially for electric cone-6 work. But it is not a universal commandment. Some commercial glazes reward a drop-and-hold or slow cool; others do not.

That is where the GPT/Grok arbitration matters most. Grok leaned toward treating Digitalfire's C6DHSC as a standard schedule for reactive cone-6 commercial glazes. GPT's research was more cautious and product-specific. The stronger editorial position for the live guide is: treat C6DHSC and similar schedules as powerful starting templates, not as universal defaults. Manufacturer guidance still has priority for a manufacturer's own line. Coyote publishes a concrete slow-cool schedule for cone 5/6 as a starting point. AMACO says Cosmos gains crystal growth with controlled cooling. But Mayco also says its stoneware crystal glazes do not require a special firing schedule to create their basic crystal effects. So the correct guidance is conditional, not absolute.

Cooling rate has real aesthetic consequences. Slow cooling promotes crystal growth, matte development, and reactive variegation, which is why it often benefits rutile blues, crystal-bearing surfaces, and some matte or variegated commercial glazes. But slow cooling can also hurt transparency. Digitalfire documents boron-rich clears that become cloudy when cooled slowly but stay clearer when cooled faster. So the safest public guidance is not "always slow cool" but "slow cool when the glaze line or test results prove it helps."

## 17. Loading, Refiring, and Commercial Caveats

Kiln loading matters more than many potters think. Skutt notes that the center of many electric kilns fires hottest, the top and bottom can underfire if packed too tightly, and thermocouple clearance and shelf spacing directly affect how heat circulates. This explains a common commercial-glaze mystery: "same glaze, same coats, different shelf, different result." Witness cones on multiple shelves are the only honest way to see this.

Refiring is the other place where casual studio advice often gets too loose. Yes, a refire can sometimes rescue minor underfire or pinholes. But Digitalfire is clear that refiring also changes the risk profile: the body is now denser and more brittle, while many glazes remelt earlier or behave more fluidly on the second pass. Some reactive glazes, especially rutile blues and self-reducing copper reds, are poor refire candidates. AMACO explicitly says that more glaze and a refire will not rescue PC-70 Copper Red once its reduction materials have burned off.

The last principle may be the most important for commercial users: label cone, maturity range, and best-looking result are not the same thing. A glaze can be rated for cone 5-6 but look best at cone 5, at cone 6, or at cone 6 with a specific cooling profile. AMACO's PC-70 Copper Red is redder at cone 5 and more purple-red and run-prone at cone 6. Mayco's SW-135 Wintergreen is more matte at cone 5 and more satin at cone 6. That is not a contradiction in the product. It is the nature of glaze response.

For the live guide, the editorial stance should therefore be:

- Emphasize oxidation-electric firing as the default commercial-glaze context.
- Explain heatwork and witness cones clearly.
- Treat drop-and-soak and slow-cool schedules as tools, not rules.
- Stress loading and venting as major hidden variables.
- Keep refiring advice cautious.
- Remind readers that the best cone is often an aesthetic target, not just the printed label.
