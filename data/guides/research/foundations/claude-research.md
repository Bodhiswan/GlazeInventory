## Section 1: Understanding Ceramic Glazes

### What a Glaze Is
**Statement:** A ceramic glaze is a thin layer of glass fused to the surface of a ceramic body through firing. It consists of three essential components: a glass-former (silica/SiO₂), a flux (which lowers the melting point — e.g., feldspar, calcium, sodium, potassium compounds), and alumina (Al₂O₃, which stiffens the melt so it doesn't run off the pot). This is fundamentally different from paint, which sits on a surface — glaze fuses into the clay body at the interface, becoming a permanent part of the object.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, 3rd ed., Krause Publications, 2000. Chapters 8-10 cover glaze composition. Also: Frank & Janet Hamer, *The Potter's Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 6th ed., A&C Black, 2015 — entry on "Glaze."
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Core ceramic science, consistent across all major textbooks and confirmed by Digitalfire glossary (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/glaze).
**Notes:** The three-component model (silica + flux + alumina) is a simplification — real glazes also contain colorants, opacifiers, and other modifiers, but this triad is the foundation of glaze chemistry.

### How Glazes Differ From Paint
**Statement:** Unlike paint, which dries by evaporation and remains a distinct surface layer, ceramic glaze undergoes a chemical transformation during firing. The raw materials melt, flow, and fuse with the clay surface at temperatures between ~900°C and 1300°C+, forming an intermediate layer where glaze and clay intermingle. Once cooled, the glaze is essentially glass bonded to ceramic — it cannot be removed without breaking the object. Paint can be applied to any dry surface; glaze application depends on the porosity of bisqueware to absorb water from the glaze suspension and deposit particles on the surface.
**Source:** Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, Ch. 8; Digitalfire glossary "glaze" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/glaze); Glazy wiki (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/firing).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Fundamental ceramic science — training data consistent with all web sources checked.
**Notes:** This distinction is important because beginners often treat glaze like paint, leading to application errors. The porosity-dependent adhesion mechanism is what makes bisque temperature so critical.

### The Role of Bisque Firing
**Statement:** Bisque firing is the first firing of raw (greenware) clay, typically to cone 08–04 (~950–1060°C / 1740–1940°F). Its purpose is to drive off all remaining moisture and chemically-bound water (quartz inversion at 573°C), burn out organic matter, and sinter the clay enough for structural strength while retaining sufficient porosity (typically >15%) to absorb glaze. Cone 04 (~1060°C) is the most common bisque temperature across studio pottery.
**Source:** Digitalfire glossary "bisque" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/bisque); BigCeramicStore "Choosing a Bisque Temperature" (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-tips-tip39_chose_bisque_temperature); Sue McLeod Ceramics "Does a lower bisque temp = more pores?" (https://suemcleodceramics.com/does-a-lower-bisque-temp-create-more-pores/).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web sources confirmed — consistent across Digitalfire, BigCeramicStore, Sue McLeod Ceramics, and Glazy.
**Notes:** The relationship is inverse: lower bisque temp = more porosity = faster glaze absorption. Some potters deliberately bisque to cone 08 for maximum porosity when using dipping glazes. For once-fire (raw glazing), bisque firing is skipped entirely — a separate technique with its own challenges.

### Cone Ranges
**Statement:** "Cone" refers to pyrometric cone numbers that measure heatwork (temperature + time), not just temperature alone. The major ranges are:
- **Low-fire:** Cone 06 to 02 (~999°C–1120°C / 1830°F–2048°F) — earthenware, bright colors, most commercial brush-on glazes
- **Mid-fire:** Cone 4 to 7 (~1162°C–1240°C / 2124°F–2264°F) — stoneware, most studio pottery today, good color range + durability
- **High-fire:** Cone 8 to 13 (~1263°C–1346°C / 2305°F–2455°F) — stoneware and porcelain, limited color palette (many colorants burn out), very durable
**Source:** Orton Ceramic Foundation cone chart (standard industry reference); Rhodes, Ch. 1-3; Glazy firing concepts (https://help.glazy.org/concepts/firing).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Cone temperatures are standardized by the Edward Orton Jr. Ceramic Foundation. Widely documented.
**Notes:** Cone numbers are NOT temperatures — they measure heatwork (thermal energy absorbed over time). A fast firing to the same peak temperature produces different results than a slow firing. Cone 6 has become the dominant range in American studio pottery due to the balance of durability, color availability, and energy efficiency (electric kilns).

## Section 2: Glaze Consistency & Measurement

### Specific Gravity Definition
**Statement:** Specific gravity (SG) is the ratio of a glaze slurry's weight to the weight of an equal volume of water. Water has SG = 1.0, so a glaze with SG 1.45 weighs 1.45 times as much as the same volume of water. SG is the primary metric for ensuring consistent glaze application — it directly correlates with the ratio of solid glaze particles to water in the suspension.
**Source:** Digitalfire glossary "specific gravity" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/specific+gravity); Glazy wiki "Specific Gravity" (https://wiki.glazy.org/t/specific-gravity/177.html); Sue McLeod Ceramics (https://suemcleodceramics.com/how-to-measure-the-specific-gravity-of-your-glazes/).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Consistent across all web sources. Standard ceramic terminology.
**Notes:** SG is sometimes called "density" in casual usage, though technically density includes units (g/mL) while SG is a dimensionless ratio.

### Specific Gravity Ranges by Application Method
**Statement:** Recommended SG ranges vary by application method:
- **Dipping:** 1.40–1.70 (most commonly 1.45–1.55 for a 1-3 second dip)
- **Pouring:** 1.50–1.60
- **Brushing:** Brush-on glazes are typically sold pre-mixed at appropriate consistency; when thinning, aim for "latex house paint" consistency
- **Spraying:** Thinner than dipping — approximately 1.25–1.40; requires constant agitation
**Source:** Digitalfire "specific gravity" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/specific+gravity); Digitalfire "dipping glaze" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/dipping+glaze); Digitalfire "pour glazing" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/pour+glazing); Glazy wiki SG page (https://wiki.glazy.org/t/specific-gravity/177.html); Rat City Studios (https://ratcitystudios.com/blog/2017/11/14/specific-gravity).
**Confidence:** High for dipping/pouring ranges. Medium for spraying (less standardized, varies more by equipment).
**Verification:** Multiple web sources cross-referenced. Digitalfire (Tony Hansen) is a widely-respected authority on glaze technology.
**Notes:** These are starting-point ranges. Every glaze formulation behaves differently — a high-clay glaze may need lower SG to avoid cracking on drying, while a low-clay glaze can tolerate higher SG. The bisque porosity also matters: more porous bisque absorbs water faster, depositing a thicker glaze layer at the same SG.

### Measurement Methods: Hydrometer vs. Weight-Per-Volume
**Statement:** Two primary methods exist for measuring SG:
1. **Hydrometer** (floating glass instrument with calibrated stem): convenient but problematic for ceramic glazes — thick/thixotropic glazes prevent the hydrometer from floating freely, leading to inaccurate readings.
2. **Weight-per-volume (graduated cylinder method):** Fill a 100cc graduated cylinder with glaze, weigh it. If 100cc weighs 145g, SG = 1.45. This is more reliable for ceramic glazes and is the preferred method among experienced potters.
The weight-per-pint method is a practical alternative: weigh a pint of glaze in grams, divide by the weight of a pint of water (473g).
**Source:** Sue McLeod Ceramics "Why I Don't Use a Hydrometer" (https://suemcleodceramics.com/why-i-dont-use-a-hydrometer-to-measure-specific-gravity/); Sue McLeod "How to Measure SG" (https://suemcleodceramics.com/how-to-measure-the-specific-gravity-of-your-glazes/); Glazy SG guide (https://help.glazy.org/testing/specific-gravity); The Studio Manager (https://www.thestudiomanager.com/posts/how-to-test-specific-gravity-in-a-ceramic-glaze-and-why-its-important); Ceramic Arts Network "How to Make a Floating Stick Hydrometer" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Make-a-Floating-Stick-Hydrometer-for-the-Cost-of-a-Milkshake).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple independent web sources agree on this recommendation.
**Notes:** Some potters make DIY floating-stick hydrometers (a dowel with a weight on one end) calibrated to their specific glaze — simpler than a lab hydrometer and works better in thick glazes. Ceramic Arts Network published a guide for this.

### Deflocculants and Flocculants
**Statement:** Glaze suspension can be chemically modified:
- **Deflocculants** disperse clay particles (electrically repel each other), reducing viscosity while maintaining solids content. Common: Darvan 7 (sodium polyelectrolyte), Darvan 811. Used in very small quantities — they are powerful. Used when you want a thin, flowable glaze with high solids content.
- **Flocculants** cause clay particles to attract and gel together, creating a thixotropic suspension that resists settling. Common: Epsom salts (magnesium sulfate — most common), vinegar (acetic acid), calcium chloride. Epsom salts are added as a saturated solution (dissolve in hot water first).
**Source:** Digitalfire "flocculation" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/flocculation); Digitalfire "deflocculation" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/deflocculation); Digitalfire "Epsom Salts" (https://digitalfire.com/material/epsom+salts); Sue McLeod "How to Make a Saturated Epsom Salt Solution" (https://suemcleodceramics.com/how-to-make-a-saturated-epsom-salt-solution-to-flocculate-your-glazes/); Linda Arbuckle "Additives for Ceramic Use" handout (https://www.lindaarbuckle.com/handouts/additives.pdf); Hardwick Handmade (https://hardwickhandmade.com/ceramic-material-clay-pottery/flocculation-deflocculation-materials-clay/).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple independent sources. Linda Arbuckle (University of Florida ceramics professor) handout is a particularly authoritative source.
**Notes:** Over-flocculating creates a glaze that gels too much and applies unevenly. Over-deflocculating creates a glaze that settles into an impenetrable hard pan at the bottom of the bucket. The goal is a glaze that stays suspended, stirs easily, and applies evenly. Vinegar works as a flocculant but its effect can be temporary as the acid gets neutralized by alkaline materials in the glaze.

### Sieving
**Statement:** Glazes should be sieved (screened) through a mesh sieve to remove lumps, undissolved particles, and contaminants. Standard practice:
- **80-mesh sieve** is the most common for stoneware and earthenware glazes (80 strands per inch)
- **100-120 mesh** for finer work
- **200+ mesh** for porcelain or glazes requiring very smooth application
- Pass the glaze through the sieve 2-3 times for best results
- For large batches (>2000g), pre-sieve through a coarser mesh (40-60) first
**Source:** Digitalfire "sieve" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/sieve); Ceramic Arts Network "Mesh Size" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/ceramic-recipes/recipe/Mesh-Size); Community discussion on sieve mesh sizes (https://community.ceramicartsdaily.org/topic/4591-what-mesh-sieve-do-you-screen-to-for-dipping-glazes/); Glazy "Mixing Test Recipes" (https://help.glazy.org/testing/mixing-tests).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Consistent across multiple web sources and ceramic community consensus.
**Notes:** Some special-effect glazes (e.g., those with coarse materials like granular manganese or ilmenite for speckling) should NOT be fully sieved, or should use a coarser mesh, to preserve the desired texture.

### Aging Glazes
**Statement:** Freshly mixed glazes benefit from aging — sitting for 24-48 hours after initial mixing allows minerals to fully hydrate, clay particles to disperse evenly, and the suspension to reach equilibrium. Some potters age glazes for weeks or months, believing longer aging produces smoother application and more consistent results. The bacterial action in aged glazes can also contribute to better suspension (organic matter from the aging process acts as a natural deflocculant/flocculant).
**Source:** Glazy "Mixing Test Recipes" (https://help.glazy.org/testing/mixing-tests); Treasureast "Ultimate Glaze Mixing Guide" (https://treasureast.com/blogs/popularize-science/ultimate-glaze-mixing-guide-techniques-recipes-and-tips-for-ceramic-artists); Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes*, Ch. 24 on glaze preparation.
**Confidence:** Medium-High (aging benefit is well-accepted practice, but the mechanism — especially the bacterial component — is debated among potters)
**Verification:** Web sources confirmed. The 24-48 hour minimum is widely recommended.
**Notes:** The analogy to clay aging is apt — both benefit from time. However, glazes with organic binders (CMC gum, gum arabic) can spoil if aged too long in warm conditions, developing mold or bad smell. Adding a few drops of bleach or using distilled water can prevent this.

## Section 3: Surface Preparation Before Glazing

### Bisque Temperature and Porosity Relationship
**Statement:** Bisque firing temperature is inversely related to porosity: lower bisque = more porous = faster glaze absorption. The practical relationship:
- **Cone 08 (~950°C):** Very porous "soft bisque." Absorbs glaze quickly — good for dipping, but pieces are fragile and absorb too fast for beginners.
- **Cone 06 (~999°C):** Still quite porous. Popular for low-fire work.
- **Cone 04 (~1060°C):** The most common bisque temperature. Good balance of strength and porosity. Works for most application methods.
- **Cone 02–1 (~1120–1137°C):** Harder bisque with lower porosity. May need dampening before glazing. Used when stronger bisque is needed for handling.
A porosity of >15% is considered ideal for glaze absorption via dipping.
**Source:** Sue McLeod Ceramics "Does a lower bisque temp = more pores?" (https://suemcleodceramics.com/does-a-lower-bisque-temp-create-more-pores/); BigCeramicStore "Choosing a Bisque Temperature" (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-tips-tip39_chose_bisque_temperature); Digitalfire "bisque" (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/bisque).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple web sources confirm the inverse relationship. >15% porosity figure from Glazy/Digitalfire.
**Notes:** The clay body also matters — a porcelain body at cone 04 will be less porous than a coarse stoneware at the same temperature. Some potters deliberately bisque lower than standard to get better glaze pickup on dense clay bodies.

### Cleaning Bisqueware
**Statement:** Bisqueware must be clean and free of dust, oil, and contaminants before glazing. Handling leaves skin oils; storage accumulates dust. Both prevent proper glaze adhesion and can cause crawling. Standard cleaning methods:
- **Damp sponge wipe:** Most common method — wipe all surfaces with a clean, damp sponge to remove dust and oils
- **Compressed air:** Blow off dust from intricate surfaces (use respirator)
- **Rinse under water:** For very dusty pieces, a quick rinse and dry
The key principle: the bisque surface must be uniformly clean so glaze adhesion is consistent across the entire piece.
**Source:** Digitalfire "crawling" (https://digitalfire.com/trouble/crawling); BigCeramicStore "Information on Glaze Problems" (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-glaze-problems); Ceramics Arts Network "Common Glaze Faults" (https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/common-glaze-faults-and-how-to-correct-them).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Universally recommended across sources. Dust/oil contamination is one of the most commonly cited causes of crawling.
**Notes:** Some potters routinely wash all bisqueware under running water and let it dry completely before glazing — this is especially important in shared studio environments where pieces may have been handled by many people.

### Wetting/Dampening the Bisque Before Application
**Statement:** Dampening bisque before glaze application slows absorption, giving more working time and allowing thinner, more even glaze layers. This is useful when:
- Bisque is very porous (low bisque temp) and absorbs glaze too quickly
- Applying glaze by brush and needing more open time
- Glazing large pieces where fast absorption would cause uneven thickness
- Applying a second layer over a first coat
The technique: lightly mist or sponge the surface with water, then apply glaze before it fully dries. For dipping, some potters quickly dip the piece in water, let it partially dry, then dip in glaze.
**Source:** BigCeramicStore "Choosing a Bisque Temperature" (https://bigceramicstore.com/pages/info-ceramics-tips-tip39_chose_bisque_temperature); Sue McLeod Ceramics (https://suemcleodceramics.com/does-a-lower-bisque-temp-create-more-pores/); community practice widely documented.
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Widely recommended technique across sources.
**Notes:** Context matters — dampening works well for porous low-fire bisque (cone 08-06) where absorption is very fast. For already-dense bisque (cone 02+), dampening can actually cause adhesion problems because there isn't enough porosity to absorb the glaze water. Rhodes notes that the bisque should be dry enough to absorb water readily but not so dry that it sucks glaze on too quickly.

### Sanding Bisque
**Statement:** Light sanding of bisqueware with fine-grit sandpaper (220-320 grit) can smooth rough surfaces, remove small bumps or kiln debris, and create a more even surface for glaze application. Sanding is done dry, and the piece must be thoroughly cleaned of sanding dust before glazing (which would otherwise cause crawling).
**Source:** General ceramic studio practice; Digitalfire (https://digitalfire.com/glossary/bisque); community consensus.
**Confidence:** Medium-High
**Verification:** Training data recall — well-established practice but less frequently written about in formal sources than other preparation methods.
**Notes:** Heavy sanding can seal the pores of the bisque surface, reducing absorption locally. Wet sanding is an alternative that reduces dust but wets the piece unevenly.

### Wax Resist on the Foot/Base
**Statement:** Applying wax resist to the bottom/foot of a pot before glazing prevents glaze from adhering to surfaces that will touch the kiln shelf. Glaze that melts onto the kiln shelf fuses the pot to the shelf, potentially ruining both. Two types of wax are commonly used:
- **Cold liquid wax resist** (water-based, commercially available — e.g., Mayco Wax Resist): easy to use, brushes clean with soap and water, most common in studios
- **Hot wax (paraffin/beeswax):** melted in a dedicated pot, applied warm. More durable resist, sharper edges, but harder to clean up and fire safety concern. Traditional method.
The wax burns off completely during firing (with some smoke — ventilate the kiln). If glaze gets on the waxed surface, it wipes off easily with a damp sponge.
**Source:** Seattle Pottery Supply "Using Wax Resist to Protect the Foot" (https://seattlepotterysupply.com/pages/using-wax-resist-to-protect-the-foot-of-a-vessel); Mayco "Wax Resist" product page (https://www.maycocolors.com/color/fired-accessories/wax-resist/); Pottery Crafters "Wax Resist Tips" (https://potterycrafters.com/wax-resist-tips/); Rat City Studios "Techniques for Using Wax Resist" (https://ratcitystudios.com/blog/2017/10/5/63bupyjlowr9v9nuainh6m03cmlmpw); Glazy wiki "Wax Resist" (https://wiki.glazy.org/t/wax-resist/265.html); Glendale Community College ceramics study guide (https://www.glendale.edu/academics/academic-divisions/visual-performing-arts-division/ceramics/study-guides/wax-resist).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Multiple independent web sources confirmed.
**Notes:** Some potters prefer to leave the foot unglazed without wax — they simply avoid glazing the bottom by holding the piece by the foot while dipping. Others use a technique called "foot wiping" where they glaze the entire piece and then clean the foot with a damp sponge. Wax is more reliable, especially for production work. For a wheel-based application of wax, placing the pot on a banding wheel and spinning it while holding a wax-loaded brush against the foot creates a clean, even wax line.
