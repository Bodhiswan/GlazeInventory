### What a Ceramic Glaze Is (Composition: Glass-Former + Flux + Alumina)
**Statement:** A ceramic glaze is a combination of raw materials that reacts to heat to produce a glass coating on ceramic pieces; it consists primarily of a glass-former (silica/SiO2, the most common), fluxes (metal oxides such as sodium, potassium, calcium that lower the maturing temperature and assist in glass formation), and alumina (Al2O3, the major viscosity agent that stabilizes the glaze, prevents excessive running, and binds it to the vertical surface of the ware).
**Source:** Mayco Colors "Ceramics 101" technical booklet (p. 3, 17; accessed via browse_page tool on https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf); IU Southeast Ceramics Studio Clay and Glaze Materials Handbook (p. 3; accessed via browse_page tool on https://claybucket.com/wp-content/uploads/materials-handbook.pdf); Linda Arbuckle Ceramic Materials handout PDF (multiple entries on silica, fluxes, alumina hydrate; accessed via browse_page tool).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer PDF and studio handbook PDFs accessed directly via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** Well-established fact across manufacturer and educational sources; alumina also sourced via clays/frits; exact ratios vary by cone range (more flux for low-fire); distinguishes from practitioner opinions.

### How Glazes Differ from Paint (Melting, Fusion to Clay Body, Permanent Transformation)
**Statement:** Unlike paint (a surface coating that does not melt or chemically fuse), a glaze is not paint but a combination of chemicals and raw materials that melts during firing to form a permanent glass layer chemically bonded/fused to the porous clay body, making the ware leak-proof, durable, and easy to clean; the unfired glaze appears dull but transforms permanently like a "butterfly" upon heat.
**Source:** Mayco Colors "Ceramics 101" technical booklet (p. 17; accessed via browse_page tool on https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer PDF accessed directly via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** Manufacturer-specific emphasis; no cone-range caveat but underfired glazes remain non-vitrified (cloudy/milky); cross-checked with Rhodes references in handbooks but primary from Mayco.

### The Role of Bisque Firing in Preparing for Glaze
**Statement:** Bisque firing burns off organic material in greenware as gas, creating a porous ware suitable for glaze absorption and adhesion; it must be fired at least one cone (preferably two) hotter than the subsequent glaze firing to prevent additional gases causing defects like pinholes/blistering during glaze fire.
**Source:** Mayco Colors "Ceramics 101" technical booklet (p. 12; accessed via browse_page tool on https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf); IU Southeast Ceramics Studio Clay and Glaze Materials Handbook (porosity/absorption definition p. 11-12; accessed via browse_page tool).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer PDF and handbook accessed directly via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** Applies across low/mid/high fire but critical for low-fire (cone 06); some high-fire porcelains bisque at cone 10 then glaze lower.

### Cone Ranges and What They Mean for Glaze Selection
**Statement:** Cone ranges indicate glaze maturation temperature: low-fire (Cone 06/05-04, e.g., many commercial glazes like Mayco Series 2000); mid-fire (Cone 4-7); high-fire (Cone 8-11); glazes must reach their labeled shelf cone exactly for proper fusion, color, and food-safety; underfired glazes appear cloudy/milky/lacking sheen.
**Source:** Mayco Colors "Ceramics 101" technical booklet (p. 5, 13; accessed via browse_page tool on https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/ceramics101.pdf); IU Southeast handbook (frit cone examples p. 48-56).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer PDF accessed directly via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** Mayco low-fire glazes mature at shelf cone 06; dinnerware-safe only when exact cone reached; varies by clay body (check manufacturer).

### Specific Gravity: What It Is, Target Ranges for Dipping, Pouring, Spraying, Brushing
**Statement:** Specific gravity (SG) is the ratio of glaze weight to the same volume of water; target ranges are approximately 1.47-1.51 for dipping Mayco dry glazes (1-sec dip), 1.40-1.70 general for dipping (lower ~1.4 for clears, higher for opaque), ~1.25-1.55 for brushing (with gums), and similar for pouring/spraying with adjustments for flow.
**Source:** Mayco dry glaze mixing technical guidance (via team cross-reference to official PDF); IU Southeast handbook and digitalfire.com practitioner summaries cross-checked with Mayco Ceramics 101 consistency notes (p. 18-19); Spectrum references ~1.50-1.70 dipping.
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Manufacturer PDFs and technical data accessed via web_search/browse_page; weight method confirmed in practitioner sources.
**Notes:** Varies by manufacturer/clay content; lower SG for brushing to avoid drag; cone-range neutral but high-iron bodies may need adjustment.

### Hydrometer vs. Weight-per-Pint Method — Pros/Cons
**Statement:** The weight-per-volume (or per-pint) method (weigh glaze, divide by volume of water in same container) is preferred for accuracy in creamy glazes; hydrometers are less reliable as they are affected by viscosity/thixotropy rather than true SG and hard to read accurately in 1.4-1.5 range typical for pottery glazes.
**Source:** Sue McLeod Ceramics article (via web_search results); digitalfire.com and team cross-references to practitioner testing.
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Web_search results confirming practitioner consensus; no direct manufacturer PDF mandates one over the other.
**Notes:** Weight method is standard "why" (direct density measure); hydrometer pros: quick but cons outweigh for viscous glazes; no cone-range dependency.

### Deflocculants (Darvan 7, Darvan 811) and Flocculants (Epsom Salts, Vinegar, Calcium Chloride) — What They Do, How Much to Use
**Statement:** Deflocculants like Darvan 7/811 (sodium polyacrylate, liquid) disperse particles to reduce water content/viscosity without thinning (typical 0.3-0.5% for porcelain, up to double for high-iron stoneware); flocculants like Epsom salts (0.1-0.5% saturated solution, or 0.2-1% dry weight) increase thixotropy/stickiness to prevent settling; Darvan advantages: less mold attack, stable slurries vs. sodium silicate/soda ash.
**Source:** IU Southeast handbook (p. 10, 44; accessed via browse_page); Vanderbilt Minerals DARVAN 811 TDS (accessed via web_search); Linda Arbuckle handout (Epsom salts flocculent entry).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer TDS and handbook PDFs accessed via tools.
**Notes:** Darvan 811 preferred for glazes/bodies over Darvan 7 in some cases; over-deflocculation thins excessively; cone-range neutral but more needed in high-clay recipes; conflicting old sources still recommend sodium silicate.

### Sieving: Recommended Mesh Sizes (80 vs. 100 vs. 200), Technique, Number of Passes
**Statement:** Glazes should be sieved/strained through 80 mesh (or finer) before use to remove lumps/grit; 200 mesh for finer materials like feldspars in recipes; typical technique: single or double pass with 80 mesh for prepared glazes.
**Source:** Mayco Ceramics 101 (p. 18: "Strain dipping glaze using 80 mesh or finer"; accessed via browse_page); IU handbook (material mesh specs e.g., 200 mesh feldspars).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original Mayco PDF accessed via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** 80 mesh standard for application; finer (100-200) for raw material prep; multiple passes if lumpy; prevents grit defects.

### Aging Glazes: Why It Helps, How Long, Mechanisms
**Statement:** Aging newly mixed glazes (days to weeks) allows particles to blend, aligns clay particles, improves suspension/thixotropy, and reduces settling; soluble materials (e.g., nepheline syenite) can cause viscosity changes over time that aging stabilizes.
**Source:** IU Southeast handbook (p. 10; accessed via browse_page); Mayco general slip/glaze aging note in Ceramics 101 (p. 4).
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Handbook PDF accessed via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** More critical for slips than glazes; mechanism is particle alignment/solubility equilibration; no strict "how long" but "prescribed time" per source.

### Bisque Temperature vs. Porosity Relationship (Cone 08 Through Cone 02 — What Changes)
**Statement:** Lower bisque temperatures (e.g., cone 08-010) produce higher porosity/absorption for better glaze uptake but more fragile ware; higher bisque (cone 04-02) reduces porosity (less absorption, sturdier piece) as fluxes begin to melt and densify the body.
**Source:** Sue McLeod Ceramics article and Ceramic Arts Daily forum summaries (via web_search); Mayco Ceramics 101 (p. 12: two-cone hotter bisque recommendation).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web_search results cross-checked with Mayco PDF.
**Notes:** Common recommendation: cone 04-06 bisque for most; cone 08 for raku/pit firing; earthenware vs. stoneware varies.

### Cleaning Bisqueware: Removing Dust, Oils, Contaminants (Methods + Why Each Matters)
**Statement:** Wipe bisqueware with a damp sponge or dusting brush before glazing to remove dust, oils, and contaminants; this prevents crawling (poor adhesion) and ensures even glaze bonding as dust/oil creates barriers.
**Source:** Mayco Ceramics 101 (p. 18: "Wipe the piece with a damp sponge before starting"; accessed via browse_page tool).
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Original manufacturer PDF accessed directly via browse_page tool.
**Notes:** Critical for all cone ranges; oils from handling are primary contaminant.

### Wetting/Dampening Bisque Before Application — When It Helps, When It Hurts, Technique
**Statement:** Dampening bisque slightly before application can help even absorption on very porous ware but is generally not recommended as it reduces glaze uptake; wipe with damp sponge (not soaking) is standard to remove dust without over-wetting.
**Source:** Mayco Ceramics 101 (p. 18 damp sponge wipe; accessed via browse_page); general practitioner consensus from search results.
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Mayco PDF verified; conflicting practitioner opinions noted.
**Notes:** Hurts on low-porosity bisque (reduces adhesion); helps only if extremely dry/dusty; technique: light damp, not wet.

### Sanding Bisque for Smoother Coats
**Statement:** Light sanding of bisque (after cleaning) produces smoother glaze coats by reducing surface irregularities that cause uneven application or texture.
**Source:** Practitioner sources cross-referenced in web_search (e.g., general glazing prep); no direct manufacturer contradiction.
**Confidence:** Low
**Verification:** No primary manufacturer PDF accessed explicitly stating sanding; based on consensus.
**Notes:** Not universally recommended (risks dust); use fine grit post-cleaning.

### Wax Resist on the Foot/Base: Hot Wax vs. Cold Liquid Wax, Application Technique, Why It Matters for Kiln Shelves
**Statement:** Wax resist (hot wax or cold liquid) applied to feet/bases prevents glaze adhesion there, protecting kiln shelves from sticking/melted glaze runs; hot wax (melted) for precise control, cold liquid for ease; apply evenly before glazing.
**Source:** Mayco product accessories (AC-302 Wax Resist referenced in search); standard in all handbooks.
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Manufacturer accessory data via web_search.
**Notes:** Essential for all firings; hot wax more durable but requires heating setup.
