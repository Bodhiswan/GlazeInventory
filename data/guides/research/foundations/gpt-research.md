### Seger Unity Formula Is a Comparison Model, Not a Batch Recipe
**Statement:** The Seger or unity formula rewrites a glaze formula so that the total of the flux oxides equals 1.00, making glaze formulas easier to compare chemically even though it does not tell the potter how many pounds or grams of raw materials to weigh out.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, pp. 172-174 (Google Books snippet results for “unity formula”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets.
**Notes:** This is the chemistry model Claude mostly implied but did not cite directly. It is useful for explaining why two glaze recipes with different raw materials can still be chemically similar.

### Unity Formula Helps Explain Fit Problems Like Crazing
**Statement:** Looking at a glaze in unity formula terms is practically useful because high-expansion fluxes can be seen more clearly once the flux column is normalized; that makes it easier to diagnose why a glaze may craze or shiver on a given body.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, pp. 172-183 (Google Books snippet results for “unity formula”); Tony Hansen, “Glaze Crazing,” Digitalfire, https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search via Google Books snippets plus direct web source.
**Notes:** This is a “why it matters” claim, not just a definition. Digitalfire is useful here because it links unity-formula chemistry to fit troubleshooting in current practice.

### Commercial Brush Glazes Often Run Much Lower SG Than Dipping Glazes
**Statement:** Commercial brushing glazes can have a much lower specific gravity than dipping glazes because gums and binders, not just solids loading, are doing much of the work of holding the glaze on the surface; Digitalfire gives a fresh low-fire brushing glaze example at SG 1.34.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Brushing Glaze,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/brushing+glaze
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippet.
**Notes:** This matters because comparing a brushing glaze’s SG directly to a dipping glaze’s SG can be misleading.

### Many Studio Dipping Glazes Work Near SG 1.43-1.45, But the Right Number Is Recipe-Specific
**Statement:** Many common partially fritted studio dipping glazes optimize around specific gravity 1.43-1.45, but the correct SG is always recipe-specific and must be tuned together with viscosity and thixotropy.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Specific gravity,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/specific+gravity; Digitalfire, “Viscosity,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/viscosity
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is a stronger and more chemistry-aware framing than giving one universal SG target.

### Manufacturer Targets Show That “Correct SG” Varies Across Product Lines
**Statement:** Manufacturer documentation shows that recommended SG varies even within commercial glaze systems: Mayco’s dry stoneware glazes target SG 1.47-1.51, while individual AMACO dipping and layering glazes can be recommended at SG 1.52, 1.53, 1.54, or even 1.58 depending on formula.
**Source:** Mayco Colors, “Mixing and Using Mayco Dry Glaze,” https://www.maycocolors.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/Mixing_and_using_mayco_dry_glazes.pdf ; AMACO product pages for DL-42 River Moss, DL-01 Ebony, DL-27 Storm Cloud, DL-60 Honeycomb, e.g. https://shop.amaco.com/dl-42-river-moss/
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets from manufacturer pages and PDFs.
**Notes:** This is a useful correction to blanket SG advice. Formula-specific rheology is normal, not an exception.

### Georgies’ Dipping Conversion Sheet Separates SG From Flow Behavior
**Statement:** Georgies’ technical sheet recommends bringing converted dipping glazes to SG 1.4-1.5, then checking flow separately: after mixing, the glaze should keep spinning for about 5 seconds, or evacuate a drip cup in roughly 15-25 seconds.
**Source:** Georgies Ceramic & Clay Co., “Converting a Brushing Glaze to Dipping,” https://www.georgies.com/pdfs/Converting%20Brush%20to%20Dip.pdf ; Georgies, “Adjusting a Glaze for Dipping,” https://www.georgies.com/pdfs/Adjusting%20Glaze%20for%20Dipping.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct PDF review and web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is valuable because SG alone does not fully describe application behavior.

### Thixotropy Means a Glaze Can Be Fluid While Moving but Gel at Rest
**Statement:** Hamer defines thixotropy as a time-dependent behavior in which a ceramic suspension is fluid when stirred or shaken but thickens or gels again on standing, making it especially useful for glaze application because the slurry can move easily in the bucket yet stay put on the pot.
**Source:** Frank and Janet Hamer, *The Potter’s Dictionary of Materials and Techniques*, 3rd ed., p. 154 (OCR text via StudyLib result already opened in session)
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web OCR source review.
**Notes:** Claude mentioned thixotropy indirectly; this gives the classic dictionary definition and why it matters in practice.

### Flocculation and Deflocculation Work by Changing Particle Charge Relationships
**Statement:** Deflocculants such as Darvan increase the effective anionic charge on particles so they repel one another and the slurry becomes more fluid at the same solids loading; flocculants such as Epsom salts or vinegar push the system back toward gelling and thixotropy.
**Source:** Vanderbilt Minerals, “DARVAN® 7-N Dispersing Agent” technical data sheet snippet, https://www.vanderbiltminerals.com/resources/TDS_DARVAN_7-N_C_and_R_Web.pdf ; Digitalfire, “Specific gravity,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/specific+gravity
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Manufacturer technical-sheet snippet plus web search snippet.
**Notes:** This is the underlying mechanism Claude only summarized generally.

### Epsom-Salt Additions Are Small and Best Added Incrementally
**Statement:** In practice, Epsom-salt additions are small: Linda Arbuckle’s cone-04 majolica handout recommends adding only 1/2-3 teaspoons of Epsom salts to 5 gallons of glaze if flocculation is needed.
**Source:** Linda Arbuckle, “Five Great Ceramic Glazing Techniques,” Ceramics Monthly/Ceramic Arts Network PDF, p. 4, https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/docs/default-source/uploadedfiles/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/fg17-5-glazingtechniques.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct PDF review.
**Notes:** This is a concrete dosage Claude did not provide. The exact amount still varies with recipe and water chemistry.

### Weight-Per-Volume Measurement Is Straightforward and Reproducible
**Statement:** Measuring glaze weight in a known volume gives an unambiguous SG value: if 100 mL of glaze weighs 145 g, the SG is 1.45; the same logic works with a marked cup if the water weight for that cup is known.
**Source:** The Studio Manager, “How to Test Specific Gravity in a Ceramic Glaze and Why It’s Important,” https://www.thestudiomanager.com/posts/how-to-test-specific-gravity-in-a-ceramic-glaze-and-why-its-important
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct webpage review.
**Notes:** This source is not a classic engineering text, but it explains the procedure clearly and concretely.

### Hydrometers Are Usable, but the Ceramic Range Is Compressed
**Statement:** Hydrometers can be used for ceramic glazes, but because many useful glaze slurries cluster tightly around SG 1.4-1.5, readings are compressed and can be hard to read accurately; that is one reason many potters prefer direct weight-per-volume measurement.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Specific gravity,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/specific+gravity ; The Studio Manager, “How to Test Specific Gravity in a Ceramic Glaze and Why It’s Important,” https://www.thestudiomanager.com/posts/how-to-test-specific-gravity-in-a-ceramic-glaze-and-why-its-important
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets plus direct webpage review.
**Notes:** This partly qualifies Claude’s strong anti-hydrometer framing: hydrometers are workable, but less informative for narrow ceramic ranges and thixotropic slurries.

### Raw Glaze Materials Are Commonly Ground Much Finer Than Most Studio Sieves
**Statement:** Rhodes notes that glaze materials are usually fine enough to pass a 200-mesh screen, which means studio sieving is often less about creating fineness from scratch and more about breaking agglomerates, catching contaminants, and re-homogenizing the slurry.
**Source:** Daniel Rhodes, *Clay and Glazes for the Potter*, p. 133 (Google Books snippet result for “dry glaze” / “200 meshes to the inch”); https://books.google.com/books?id=9ed8CwAAQBAJ
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Web search snippet via Google Books.
**Notes:** This does not replace Claude’s 80/100/120-mesh studio sieve guidance; it explains why those studio screens can still work even though the raw powders are much finer.

### Cone-04 Is Common, but “Enough Porosity” Depends on the Body
**Statement:** For low-fire terra cotta, fired body porosity around cone 06-04 is often still in the 10-15% range, which is why these bodies can accept glaze readily; but highly vitrifying specialty bodies may reach near-zero porosity by cone 04 and therefore need a much lower bisque.
**Source:** Digitalfire, “Leaking of Fired Ceramics,” https://digitalfire.com/trouble/leaking+of+fired+ceramics ; Digitalfire, “Zero4,” https://digitalfire.com/glossary/zero4
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** This is an important caveat to generic bisque advice. Body composition matters as much as cone number.

### Lower Bisque Improves Absorption but Trades Away Strength
**Statement:** Digitalfire specifically notes that many terra-cotta bodies work better with dipping glazes when bisque-fired fairly low, such as cone 06, because more porosity remains; practical studio guidance likewise treats cone 06 bisque as more porous but more fragile than cone 04 bisque.
**Source:** Digitalfire, recipe G1916Q snippet, https://www.digitalfire.com/recipe/g1916q ; Pottery Crafters, “What Temperature Do I Bisque Fire Pottery Clay To?” snippet via DuckDuckGo search
**Confidence:** Medium-High
**Verification:** Web search snippets.
**Notes:** The exact absorption percentage depends strongly on body formulation, not just temperature.

### Dampening Can Help Pickup, but Over-Wetting Can Trigger Crawling
**Statement:** Slightly dampening bisque before dipping can improve glaze pickup and suppress dust, but too much water can move the raw glaze layer, encourage color migration, and increase crawling risk.
**Source:** Linda Arbuckle, “Five Great Ceramic Glazing Techniques,” pp. 4-5, https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/docs/default-source/uploadedfiles/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/fg17-5-glazingtechniques.pdf
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Direct PDF review.
**Notes:** This is a needed caveat to “dampen the bisque” advice. It helps especially with stiff low-fire base glazes like majolica, but it is not universally beneficial.

### Cold Wax Resist Is the Current Studio Default Because It Solves Most Foot-Waxing Needs More Safely
**Statement:** Water-based cold wax resist is now the routine studio choice for waxing feet because it repels glaze effectively, burns away in firing, and avoids the handling hazards of melted hot wax; Mayco explicitly recommends it for waxing the foot of thrown pieces.
**Source:** Mayco Colors, “Wax Resist,” https://www.maycocolors.com/color/fired-accessories/wax-resist/ ; Ceramic Arts Network/Pottery Making Illustrated, “A Cooler Alternative,” snippet via DuckDuckGo search
**Confidence:** High
**Verification:** Manufacturer page snippet plus practitioner article snippet.
**Notes:** Hot wax still gives a crisp line and some production potters prefer it, but the safety and cleanup tradeoff is real.

### Terra Sigillata Can Function as an Intentional Base Layer Under Glaze
**Statement:** Terra sigillata is not only a standalone burnished finish; some commercial ceramic suppliers explicitly recommend it as a very fine engobe “best under a glaze,” making it a legitimate specialized base-layer choice rather than only a historical surface treatment.
**Source:** Walker Ceramics Australia, “Terra Sigillata,” https://shop.walkerceramics.com.au/AD4976.1/Terra-Sigillata/pd.php
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Web search snippet.
**Notes:** This is a bonus/uncommon prep technique. Performance depends on the clay body, firing range, and the glaze layered over it.

### Slip Can Also Be Used at Bisque Stage, but Fit Matters
**Statement:** Slip decoration is not strictly limited to leather-hard application; Ceramic Arts Network notes slips can be applied in the bisque state for thin, skin-like surfaces, but because slips are clay-rich they still need compatible shrinkage and firing behavior if they are going under glaze.
**Source:** Ceramic Arts Network, “How to Decorate Pottery Surfaces with Slips in the Bisque State,” https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/How-to-Decorate-Pottery-Surfaces-with-Slips-in-the-Bisque-State
**Confidence:** Medium
**Verification:** Web search snippet.
**Notes:** This is another uncommon prep route. It is much more context-dependent than ordinary bisque glazing.
